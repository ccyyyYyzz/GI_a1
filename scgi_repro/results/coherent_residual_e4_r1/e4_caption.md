# E4 Coherent Residual-Gain Validation of Theorem 1 (r1)

Setup: three reconstructors (orthogonal_inverse, srht_inverse, random_dgi) at K=N=1024, 10 objects x 10 seeds x 96 inner MC draws/seed, fixed residual-gain variance v=0.001, five injected covariance structures for delta_n: iid, AR(1) phi=0.90, sinusoidal (period N/8), low-pass-smooth OU (rho=1e-03), and blockwise-constant (block=64). Measured relMSE is the residual-gain-only contribution recon(delta)-recon(delta=0), normalized by S2=sum(object^2) -- not the scale-aligned rel_mse() of Fig. 4 -- so it isolates Theorem 1's gain term from the bias term and the alignment gauge freedom. Each seed's measured value is itself an average over 96 independent delta draws (Theorem 1's gain term is an expectation over delta; coherent structures like sinusoidal/blockwise have few effective degrees of freedom per single draw, so inner-draw averaging is needed for a low-noise per-seed estimate).
Scalar law (v*B_L): near-exact for iid (worst |ratio, 1/ratio|=1.044 across all basis/object cells). For orthogonal_inverse and srht_inverse, G2=L^T L is analytically proportional to the identity (I/N) for a complete orthogonal inverse, so the gain term depends only on diag(V_delta)=v regardless of off-diagonal structure -- scalar and matrix laws coincide exactly at v for every structure on these two bases (worst coherent-structure ratio on orthogonal/srht = 1.044). The scalar law's coherent-residual failure is therefore basis-dependent: it FAILS specifically for random_dgi, whose correlation-based reconstruction kernel L^T L is not isotropic -- worst coherent-structure ratio across all cells = 3.652 (random_dgi-only worst = 3.652).
Matrix law (tr(L diag(b) V_delta diag(b) L^T)/S2, true V_delta): stays within 4.22% of measured everywhere in this panel (max |measured-predicted|/predicted = 0.0422, at basis=orthogonal_inverse, structure=blockwise, object=digit_2) -- consistent with the ~10% target and validating Theorem 1's coherent-residual clause as an identity, not an approximation.
