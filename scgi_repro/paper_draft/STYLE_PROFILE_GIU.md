# Wen Chen Group (PolyU) — Authoritative Writing Style Profile

**Corpus synthesized:** 8 papers, 2022–2026. Letters/articles: Xiao 2022 (OL), Zhou 2023 (OE), Peng 2024 (APL), Peng 2025 (OE), Song 2025 (OL), Hao 2025 (APL Photonics). Reviews: Xiao & Chen 2025 (APR — cleanest solo group voice), Jiao et al. 2026 (Adv. Imaging — diluted multi-lab voice, quarantined below). Conflicts resolved by frequency and recency: 2023–2025 letters/articles are the canonical register; the 2022 OL paper's absolutist tics ("totally cannot work") are treated as early-career deviation; the reviews' survey voice is reported separately and never merged into the research-paper register.

---

## 1. Voice Summary

The Chen group writes in a heavily agentless passive register (~70–80% of methods/results sentences) in which "we" appears only at two fixed hinges — the "In this paper/Letter, we report/propose" pivot and the "we have reported" conclusion opener — while everything else is carried by impersonal frames, above all the workhorse "It is (experimentally) demonstrated that …". The prose adversary and the hero are both fixed noun phrases repeated verbatim, never paraphrased: "dynamic and complex scattering media" versus "the proposed method," with quality always expressed through "high-X" compounds (high-resolution, high-fidelity, high-robustness) and quantities marshaled by "a series of." Every displayed relation is numbered and introduced by a "can be described by"-family clause, then immediately glossed by a single exhaustive "where X denotes …, Y denotes …" sentence. Claims about their own results are asserted flatly in present tense and anchored to numbers, while limitations are displaced onto conventional/competing methods or folded into stated assumptions; hedging modals (could, would, "It can be believed that") are reserved for mechanism caveats and the forward-looking close. The architecture is invariant: a funnel introduction ending in the "In this paper, we …" hinge with prose (never bulleted) contributions, figure-anchored results paragraphs threaded by "as shown in Fig. n" / "It can be observed that," and a present-perfect conclusion closing on the signature "opens (up) an avenue for …" outlook.

## 3. Sentence & Paragraph Mechanics

- **Length mix:** medium-long declaratives dominate (20–35 words, one qualifying clause), punctuated by short verdict sentences ("Therefore, conventional GI methods are not applicable."). Rhythm = long setup → short punch.
- **Voice:** 70–80% agentless passive in methods/results ("optical experiments are conducted," "an iterative algorithm is developed"). "We" only at the intro hinge and the conclusion opener. Active voice otherwise given to the anthropomorphized method ("The proposed method can supervise and self-correct …").
- **Sentence openings:** topic-/condition-fronted is the default ("When dynamic and complex scattering media exist, …"; "As water turbidity increases, …"; "To eliminate the effect of X, …" purpose-fronting). Expletive clefts ("It is a challenge to …," "It is desirable to …") are a hallmark.
- **Cohesion:** by lexical repetition, not connective variety — consecutive sentences restart with the same key noun. Sentence-initial "Therefore, / However, / Then, / Moreover, / In addition," carry the logic.
- **Paragraphs:** monotopic, 5–10 sentences; intro paragraphs longest (8–14); results paragraphs one-figure-each, opened by the figure or condition and closed by the interpretive consequence.

---

## 4. Equation Presentation Rules

1. **Lead-in:** a full clause ending in "by" (no colon), then the display: "The measurements … can be described by" → Eq. (n). Variants: "is described by," "can be obtained by," "is calculated by," "as defined by"; terse derivation chaining uses "Then, we have."
2. **The "where" gloss (invariant):** every display equation is immediately followed by one run-on "where" sentence defining **every** symbol in order, comma-separated, verb "denotes" (paired symbols: "X and Y respectively denote …"). Parenthetical "(i.e., …)" glosses may be embedded.
3. **Numbering density:** near-total — every relation of interest, including metric definitions and trivial constraints, gets a numbered display. Inline math limited to single symbols.
4. **Back-reference:** always "Eq. (n)" / "Eqs. (m)–(n)"; sentence-initial spelled out ("Equation (9) can be re-written as"). Interpretive back-reference frames: "It can be found in Eq. (n) that …," "In Eq. (n), …," "Applying Eq. (m) into Eq. (n), we have."
5. **Plain-language restatement:** important equations get an "It is given in Eq. (n) that ⟨restatement⟩" sentence.

---

## 5. Claim / Limitation Phrasing

**Claims:**
- Own results: present tense, impersonal-passive frame, no hedge on the win — "It is experimentally demonstrated that the proposed method is feasible and robust against ⟨X⟩." Strength lives in the adverb ("effectively," "always"), not bare intensifiers.
- Every quality claim is number-anchored ("spatial resolution of 44.19 µm is achieved, approaching the diffraction limit (40.0 µm)"). Superlatives are earned with a metric.
- Comparative device: always paired against the fixed foil, stated flatly, never gloating — "… where conventional methods could not work"; "the CNR is always close to 0 in conventional GI."
- Novelty: at most one factual "This is the first attempt to …" or "a new method, to the best of our knowledge."
- Tense: present for standing results; present-perfect for the paper's own act ("we have reported").

**Limitations:**
- Displaced onto conventional/prior methods (the gap being closed), or onto a deliberately weaker variant of their own scheme to elevate the main one.
- Own-method limits are folded into stated assumptions ("It has been assumed that the estimation in Eq. (3) is accurate in order to have k̂ᵢ ≈ kᵢ") or physically justified boundaries ("as the attenuation coefficient increases beyond ⟨value⟩, … the PSNR falls below 30.0 dB"), never apologetic and never in a "Limitations" section.
- Defensive-justification move: "The fluctuations are justified, because ⟨metric property⟩."

## 6. Figure Caption Formula

`[Noun-phrase title, no verb]. (a) ⟨panel content⟩, (b) ⟨panel content⟩, … ⟨metric values⟩, respectively. ABBR: expansion; ABBR: expansion; …`

- Captions **label and enumerate; they never interpret** — interpretation lives in body text.
- Panel glosses are parallel noun phrases; cross-links via "corresponding to (a)."
- Setup figures carry the semicolon-separated abbreviation glossary ("SLM: spatial light modulator; SPD: single-pixel detector; …").
- Metric dumps live in the caption ("PSNR values are 12.50 dB, 12.62 dB, … respectively.").
- Timeless present / verbless; occasional practical aside ("For the sake of brevity, … is omitted.").

## 7. Intro Architecture Template

Funnel of 3–5 paragraphs, fixed move order, **no bulleted contributions**:

1. **¶1 — Field importance + incumbent + its ceiling.** Broad opener elevating the field/technique, citation-dense pedigree, then the soft complaint: "However, ⟨incumbent⟩ could be insufficient to … / could not work properly …"
2. **¶2 — Hero technique + literature tour + narrowing.** Define the technique, survey sub-methods with "e.g." lists and bracketed numerals, then confine: "substantial progress … is confined primarily to ⟨easier setting⟩."
3. **¶3 — Specific gap + desirability.** "However, ⟨task⟩ remains an open question / remains challenging. … Therefore, it is (always) desirable to ⟨need⟩." Prior attempts get credit-then-"but"; own prior work may be critiqued as the immediate gap ("Although ⟨our prior⟩ is feasible, ⟨specific weakness⟩.").
4. **¶4 — The hinge + mechanism + promise.** "In this paper/Letter, we report/propose ⟨method⟩ (⟨ACRONYM⟩) to ⟨goal⟩ …" followed by 2–4 prose sentences walking the pipeline (each component "is developed / is designed and applied"), optionally ordinal enumeration ("Firstly, … Secondly, … Thirdly, …"), closing on the verification promise: "To verify the developed method, a series of optical experiments are conducted …" or the outlook "It is expected that an avenue could be opened for ⟨goal⟩."

## 8. Conclusion Formula

1. **Opener (fixed):** "In conclusion, we have reported / In this paper, we have reported ⟨headline⟩ …" (present-perfect; occasionally fully agent-suppressed: "In conclusion, a new ⟨X⟩ method has been reported for …").
2. **Body (3–6 sentences):** restate mechanism components ("The ⟨A⟩ and ⟨B⟩ are developed to …") + restate validation with headline numbers, reusing "It is (experimentally) demonstrated that …" verbatim.
3. **Applications sentence:** "the proposed method … is applicable in various complex environments (e.g., ⟨examples⟩)."
4. **Closing sentence (signature, pick one):**
   - "The proposed ⟨method⟩ opens (up) an avenue for ⟨capability⟩ in ⟨environments⟩, e.g., ⟨example⟩ / with a potential to ⟨broader reach⟩."
   - "It can be believed that the proposed method provides a novel perspective for ⟨application⟩ in various ⟨settings⟩."
   - "The proposed ⟨method⟩ provides a solution for ⟨capability⟩ in dynamic and complex scattering environments."
   - "⟨Method⟩ would represent a key step towards applications of ⟨field⟩ …" / "could give an impetus to ⟨development⟩."

## 9. Transition Bank

- **Logical:** Therefore, · However, (the gap/limitation pivot, always sentence-initial) · Then, · Moreover, · In addition, · Furthermore, · In contrast, · Finally, · Since ⟨premise⟩, · As a result, · Based on the above analyses, · In other words, · To be specific, · For instance, · Despite these achievements,
- **Purpose-fronting:** "To ⟨verb⟩, ⟨clause⟩ …" ("To eliminate ⟨problem⟩, an ⟨adj⟩ algorithm is developed to …"; "To evaluate ⟨property⟩, ⟨metrics⟩ are used.")
- **Figure/result spine:** "… is/are shown in Fig. n." · "As shown in Fig. n, …" · "As can be seen in Figs. …" · "It can be observed/seen that …" · "Figures n(a) and n(b) show …" · "⟨Parameter⟩ is also studied/investigated, as shown in Fig. n."
- **Topic transition (review-flavored but usable):** "In terms of ⟨topic⟩," · "It is worth noting that ⟨qualification⟩."
- **Section hinge:** "In this paper/Letter, we report/propose …" (intro→method); "In conclusion, …"

## 11. APPLICABILITY MAP — theory/identifiability manuscript for IEEE-TCI (theorems + proofs)

Decisive rulings. "ADOPT" = use as-is; "ADAPT" = use with the stated modification; "SKIP" = would damage the theorem register or violate IEEE-TCI norms.

| # | Style feature | Ruling | Instruction |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Agentless passive at 70–80% | **ADAPT** | Target ~40–50%. IEEE-TCI theory papers use "we" freely for mathematical acts ("we prove," "we show," "we now bound"). Keep passive for setup/definitions ("the measurement process is modeled as"); use active "we" in proofs and derivation logic. Full Chen-level passivity makes proofs unreadable. |
| 2 | "In this paper, we propose/report …" hinge + prose (non-bulleted) contributions | **ADAPT** | Keep the hinge sentence verbatim ("In this paper, we …"). But IEEE-TCI expects an explicit contribution enumeration — use the Peng ordinal variant ("Firstly, … Secondly, … Thirdly, …") in prose, or a short itemized list; the ordinal-prose form is the Chen-compatible compromise. |
| 3 | "It is demonstrated that" claim frame | **ADAPT** | Replace with theorem-anchored equivalents: "Theorem 2 establishes that …," "It is shown in Theorem 2 that …," "It is proved that …". Reserve "It is demonstrated that" for the numerical-experiments section only. Never use "demonstrated" for a mathematical result — use *proved/established/shown*. |
| 4 | "It is experimentally demonstrated/verified/validated that" | **ADAPT** | Use only in the numerics section, downgraded to "It is numerically verified that ⟨theoretical prediction⟩" / "Numerical results validate the tightness of the bound in Eq. (n)." SKIP everywhere else. |
| 5 | "can be described by" equation lead-in + comma, no colon | **ADOPT** | Fits theory prose perfectly: "The single-pixel acquisition can be described by" → Eq. Also use variants "is given by," "can be written as" for derivation steps. |
| 6 | Exhaustive "where X denotes …" gloss after every display | **ADOPT** | This is the single easiest and highest-yield Chen fingerprint, and it is fully compatible with theorem style. Gloss every new symbol immediately, verb "denotes," "respectively denote" for pairs. Exempt only in-proof algebra where symbols were already defined. |
| 7 | Number every displayed relation, minimal inline math | **ADAPT** | Number every relation that is *referenced later* or *stated in a theorem*; but a theory paper may leave unreferenced in-proof steps unnumbered per IEEE convention. Do not number trivialities (Chen's "Eq. (3): Pk = Xk" habit reads amateurish in TCI). |
| 8 | Back-reference forms "In Eq. (n)," / "It can be found in Eq. (n) that …" / "Applying Eq. (m) into Eq. (n), we have" | **ADOPT** | Use verbatim; extend the same pattern to theorems ("It follows from Theorem 1 and Eq. (n) that …"). |
| 9 | "It is given in Eq. (n) that ⟨plain restatement⟩" | **ADOPT** | Excellent for making theorems legible — restate each theorem's inequality in words immediately after the statement. |
| 10 | "It is worth noting that ⟨qualification⟩" | **ADOPT** | Maps directly onto Remark environments: "Remark 1. It is worth noting that …". Use for scope extensions and assumption discussion. |
| 11 | "a series of" | **ADOPT** | Natural for measurement ensembles: "a series of illumination patterns," "a series of realizations." Use freely; it signals house voice at zero cost. |
| 12 | "the proposed method/framework" self-label, never "our method" | **ADOPT** | Use "the proposed framework / the proposed identifiability conditions" throughout. |
| 13 | "high-X" compound adjectives (high-fidelity, high-resolution) | **ADAPT** | Use sparingly and only for imaging quantities the theory speaks about ("high-fidelity reconstruction"). Do NOT coin theory compounds ("high-identifiability"); for mathematical properties use standard terms (tight, sharp, minimax-optimal, necessary and sufficient). |
| 14 | Fixed adversary phrase "dynamic and complex scattering media," repeated verbatim | **ADAPT** | Adopt the *mechanism* — pick ONE fixed noun phrase for our adversary (e.g., "unknown measurement-basis perturbations" or whatever the paper's nuisance is) and repeat it verbatim, never paraphrasing. Do not import Chen's literal scattering phrase unless the setting genuinely involves scattering. |
| 15 | Foil device "where conventional methods could not work" | **ADAPT** | Theory version: "where conventional second-order correlation reconstruction is not identifiable" / "conditions under which conventional GI provably fails." Keep the fixed-foil rhythm; ground the failure in a proposition, not a CNR value. |
| 16 | Quantitative anchoring of every claim (CNR/PSNR dumps) | **ADAPT** | Anchor claims to theorem numbers, rates, and constants instead ("with rate N⁻¹/², matching the minimax lower bound in Theorem 3"). Metric dumps belong only in the numerics section. |
| 17 | Limitations displaced onto conventional methods; own limits as assumptions | **ADAPT** | Half-adopt: framing prior theory as "incomplete" (Xiao 2022's move) is *exactly* our genre and should be used. But IEEE-TCI theory reviewers expect explicit assumption/scope honesty — state our assumptions in a dedicated Assumptions block and discuss sharpness/necessity; do not bury them Chen-style. Use "It has been assumed that ⟨A⟩ in order to have ⟨B⟩" as the connective. |
| 18 | Hedging modals on own results ("can be applied to," "could offer") | **SKIP** | Theorems are not hedged. State proved results flatly ("Theorem 2 shows that N ≥ K + p is necessary"). Reserve "could/would" strictly for the outlook paragraph and conjectures (label conjectures as such). |
| 19 | "It can be believed that …" | **SKIP** | Non-idiomatic and too soft for IEEE-TCI; a reviewer will flag it. Replace the closing hedge with "We expect that …" or the avenue formula (#26 below). |
| 20 | Absolutist intensifiers ("totally cannot work," "super-high," "unprecedented robustness," "always" as hype) | **SKIP** | Early/letter-register tics; poisonous next to proofs. "Always" is permitted only when it is literally a quantifier ("for all N ≥ K + p"). |
| 21 | Topic-/condition-fronted sentences; long-setup → short-verdict rhythm | **ADOPT** | Fits theorem prose naturally ("When the stationarity anchor is absent, identifiability fails."). Use short verdict sentences after long derivations. |
| 22 | Cohesion by lexical repetition of the key noun | **ADOPT** | Ideal for theory: repeat the defined term exactly (never synonymize a defined object). This coincides with mathematical-writing best practice. |
| 23 | Transition bank (Therefore, However, Then, Moreover, In addition, To ⟨verb⟩ …, Since …) | **ADOPT** | Use verbatim; "Therefore," and "Then," are natural derivation connectives. Add proof-specific ones (Hence, It follows that, Combining Eqs. (m)–(n)) which are register-compatible. |

One avenue sentence only; no "impetus" stacking. |
| 27 | Intro funnel (field → foil → gap → hinge → mechanism prose) | **ADOPT** | Use the 4-paragraph funnel exactly, with the gap stated theoretically (Xiao 2022 is the in-corpus precedent: "the theoretical description … is still incomplete"). This is the strongest structural alignment signal for a Chen-collaboration-adjacent submission. |
| 28 | "It is desirable to ⟨need⟩" gap sentence | **ADOPT** | Verbatim: "Therefore, it is desirable to establish conditions under which ⟨object⟩ can be uniquely identified." |
| 29 | Caption formula (noun-phrase title + panel enumeration + no interpretation + glossary) | **ADOPT** | Apply to all figures/plots: "Identifiability phase diagram for ⟨model⟩. (a) ⟨panel⟩, (b) ⟨panel⟩. ⟨parameter values⟩, respectively." Keep interpretation in body text. Extend the same discipline to tables. |
| 30 | Metric-values-in-caption dumps | **ADAPT** | For numerics figures only; report simulation parameters and key values in the caption. Skip for schematic/theory figures. |
| 31 | Abstract style: one dense block, "high-X" pairs, mechanism + validation promise | **ADAPT** | Keep the dense single-block form and the "In this paper, we …" spine, but the validation clause becomes "Necessary and sufficient conditions … are established, and minimax rates are derived," with numerics as the final sentence. |
| 32 | No bulleted lists, no em-dashes, no rhetorical questions, bracketed-numeral citations only | **ADOPT** | Matches IEEE norms anyway. Exception per #2: a compact contribution list is permitted. |
| 33 | Boilerplate blocks (Funding/Disclosures/Data availability) | **ADAPT** | Replace with IEEE-TCI equivalents (Acknowledgment, standard IEEE structure); keep the "data … may be obtained from the authors upon reasonable request" sentence if applicable to code/artifacts. |
| 34 | Review-register features (agentive "In 2008, Shapiro introduced …", "roadmap," "we systematically categorize," title-echo close) | **SKIP** | Survey voice only; do not import into a research manuscript. Exception: the related-work subsection may use one or two agentive historical sentences if a chronological literature tour is needed — that is the only sanctioned leak. |
| 35 | Novelty flag "This is the first attempt to …" / "to the best of our knowledge" | **ADAPT** | Use at most once, attached to the central theoretical claim: "To the best of our knowledge, this is the first identifiability characterization of ⟨setting⟩." |
