\section{Introduction}

Single-pixel (ghost) imaging \cite{Pittman1995PRA,Bennink2002PRL,Shapiro2008PRA,Bromberg2009PRA} has become the method of choice for imaging through media that defeat conventional cameras \cite{Bina2013PRL,Cheng2009OE}: turbid water, fog, smoke, dynamic scattering media \cite{PengChen2024APL}, and complex channels around corners \cite{PengXiaoChen2025OE}. The instrument is disarmingly simple---a series of structured illumination patterns and a bucket detector \cite{Duarte2008SPM,Edgar2019NatPhoton}---and the textbook design instinct is equally simple: use an ordered orthogonal basis. A Hadamard scan acquires exactly one transform coefficient per frame \cite{Zhang2017OE,Yu2019Sensors}, the measurement matrix is perfectly conditioned, and an exact inverse is waiting at the end of the acquisition. Against additive noise, this is provably the safe choice; nothing about the pattern set is left to chance.

Dynamic media break this instinct in a specific and, as will be argued, instructive way. A time-varying channel multiplies the bucket record by a slowly drifting gain: the $n$-th measurement is $R_n = a_n B_n$, where $B_n$ is the object-dependent bucket coefficient and $a_n > 0$ is an unknown per-frame gain with log-gain $\ell_n = \log a_n$ confined to a slow-drift class $S$ of dimension $p$. Recent experimental work on ghost imaging through dynamic and complex scattering media \cite{PengChen2024APL} shows that a self-calibrating scheme built on \emph{random} illumination with a supervised correction of dynamic scaling factors succeeds where ordered scans fail, and companion work on optical wireless transmission around corners \cite{PengXiaoChen2025OE} relies on the same random-pattern robustness for data encoding through complex channels. Read naively, this is a puzzle: the ordered Hadamard scan is the better-conditioned instrument, yet randomness---usually a concession, not a feature---wins. Therefore, it is desirable to determine when, and by what mechanism, a drifting gain becomes blindly separable at all.

The resolution is that under gain drift the ordering itself becomes the liability. An ordered scan sorts the object's transform coefficients into a smooth sequence in acquisition time, so the object acquires a \emph{temporal signature}: a slow gain trace and a slow coefficient trace cast the same shadow on the one-dimensional bucket record, and no estimator can tell them apart from that record alone. Hadamard is not defeated by randomness; it is defeated by chronology. It is shown that this confounding is not an artifact of any particular correction algorithm but a genuine algebraic ambiguity---for a square unconstrained design, \emph{any} invertible pattern matrix can absorb a nonconstant gain into a perfectly valid alternative object (Theorem~A). Conversely, random carriers escape it by a statistical mechanism: when the pattern sequence makes the bucket carrier's distribution stationary in time, slow gain becomes the only non-stationary component of the record, and the relative gain becomes identifiable up to the unavoidable global scale (Theorem~B). Self-calibration is thus a property one can \emph{design into acquisition time}, and its price, rates, and failure boundaries can be computed in advance.

In this paper, we develop that observation into a quantitative identifiability theory. The contributions are:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{A three-notion identifiability framework} for the bilinear gain--object model $R_n = a_n B_n$, separating exact-algebraic identifiability, statistical (asymptotic) identifiability, and estimation conditioning. Within it, the square-design obstruction (Theorem~A) is proved, and the tall-design thresholds (Theorem~A$'$) are proved for every fixed drift class $S$ containing the constants: generic exact identifiability at $N \ge K + p$ for almost every design--object pair, uniform identifiability over every object with nonvanishing carrier at $N \ge 2K + p - 1$, and a generic local threshold at $N = K + p - 1$ that is an if-and-only-if for $K \ge 2$, with genuine positive-dimensional alias families below it (Secs.~4, 9). This is distinct from generic bilinear-injectivity results \cite{KechKrahmer1603.07316,LiLeeBresler1501.06120,AhmedRechtRomberg1211.5608,ChoudharyMitra1402.2637,KuengRauhutTerstiege1410.6913}: the operator here is a diagonal-row map, and the anchor is temporal, not structural.

\item \textbf{The stationarity anchor (Theorem~B)} and its consistency condition ($\star$): random carriers pin the relative gain statistically, with a windowed log-domain estimator achieving the minimax rate over the drift class, including a photon-limited variant whose error scales as $1/(W\bar\lambda)$ for windows $W$.

\item \textbf{A synthesis, not a trade-off:} randomized Hadamard (SRHT) designs retain orthogonal conditioning while randomizing the temporal carrier --- the stationary/exchangeable carrier being supplied under Walsh-flatness of $T^2$ or a successful random permutation of a sufficiently spread object --- with an exact Walsh-domain necessary-and-sufficient whitening condition separating the roles of sign flips and permutations.

\item \textbf{An exact finite-noise reconstruction bridge} (Theorem~1) expressing relative MSE for any linear reconstructor through a leverage functional $B_L$, covering residual gain, read noise, and Poisson noise without Gaussian approximation under explicit exogenous-gain-estimate hypotheses, and specializing to orthogonal, pairwise-Hadamard, and random/DGI pipelines \cite{Ferri2010PRL,Sun2012OE} (with the DGI constants measured per implementation---no universal value exists, and the sampling-floor and drift-leverage constants of one pipeline differ once a background is subtracted).

\item \textbf{A design chart with a finite-$N$ flip boundary} $\rho^*$ in the drift-decorrelation parameter $\rho_{\mathrm{pair}}$ (Prop~3), giving a pre-acquisition go/no-go rule (Fig.~5) for choosing between ordered and randomized acquisition; the boundary's gain-known skeleton is validated with no free parameters, and its corrected blind-arm form predicts --- and the data confirm --- no flip for mean-subtracted blind DGI pipelines on the simulated grid.
\end{enumerate}

The scope is emphasized: all results are theoretical, supported by numerical evidence; no hardware validation is reported. Identifiability near $N = K + p$ is a uniqueness statement, not a conditioning guarantee, and random square designs remain algebraically non-identifiable---their advantage is statistical. The organizing idea throughout is the distilled form of the paradox: one makes the gain observable not by measuring it directly, but by making the object stop pretending to be time.

The paper is organized as follows. Section~2 fixes the model and the three notions of identifiability; Fig.~1 places ordered-Hadamard, iid-random, tall-random, and SRHT designs on the resulting two-axis map of conditioning versus identifiability. Section~3 positions the work against bilinear inverse-problem theory. Sections~4 and 5 establish the algebraic obstruction and the stationarity anchor; Sec.~6 derives estimation rates including the low-photon regime. Section~7 develops the SRHT synthesis, Sec.~8 the reconstruction bridge and phase diagram. Section~9 presents the numerical experiments, and Sec.~10 collects design rules, scope, and outlook.

\section{Problem setup and three notions of identifiability}

We consider a single-pixel (bucket-detection) acquisition of $N$ frames. On frame $n$ a programmed pattern $m_n \in \mathbb{R}^K$ illuminates a static object $T \in \mathbb{R}^K$, producing the ideal bucket value $B_n = m_n^{\mathsf T} T$; we call the sequence $\{B_n\}$ the \emph{object carrier}. The recorded signal is corrupted by a slowly drifting multiplicative gain (source power, detector responsivity, coupling efficiency) and can be described by

\[
R_n \;=\; a_n B_n, \qquad a_n = e^{\ell_n} > 0 ,
\]

where $a_n$ denotes the unknown per-frame gain and the log-gain trajectory $\ell = (\ell_1,\dots,\ell_N)$ is confined to a \emph{drift class} $S \subset \mathbb{R}^N$, $p = \dim S$, which always contains the constants. The constant direction is a pure gauge: $(a, T) \mapsto (c\,a,\; T/c)$ leaves every $R_n$ invariant, so all statements below are up to a global scale. For physically slow drift we take $S$ to be a low-pass (Fourier) space of normalized bandwidth $\rho_{\mathrm{bw}}$, so that $p \approx \rho_{\mathrm{bw}} N$; this bandwidth parameter must not be confused with the adjacent-pair decorrelation $\rho_{\mathrm{pair}}$ entering the finite-$N$ flip boundary of Sec.~8. The blind problem --- recover $(a, T)$ from $\{R_n\}$ alone --- is bilinear, and asking whether it is ``solvable'' turns out to be three distinct questions. Separating them is itself a contribution of this paper.

\begin{nameddef}{Conventions (fixed throughout)}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
\item $K$, $T$ --- number of pixels; object $T\in\mathbb{R}^K$, entrywise nonnegative unless stated otherwise.
\item $N$ --- number of acquired frames.
\item $S$ --- linear log-gain drift class; \textbf{always contains the constants}.
\item $p=\dim S$ --- drift dimension; the \emph{nonconstant} drift directions number $p-1$.
\item global scale --- \textbf{all} identifiability statements are modulo the gauge $(a,T)\mapsto(c\,a,\,T/c)$; we fix the gauge by centering the log-gain ($\overline{\ell}=0$); reconstruction error metrics use least-squares scale alignment (Sec.~8).
\item identifiability notions --- \emph{local-differential}, \emph{generic-exact}, and \emph{uniform-exact} identifiability are distinct properties with distinct thresholds (Theorem~A$'$); none of them implies conditioning.
\item $\rho_{\mathrm{bw}}$ --- normalized gain \textbf{bandwidth}: $p\approx\rho_{\mathrm{bw}}N$ (tall-design law, Sec.~4).
\item $\rho_{\mathrm{pair}}$ --- \textbf{adjacent-pair} gain decorrelation, entering $\mathrm{Var}(\Delta)\approx 2s^2 r(\rho_{\mathrm{pair}})$ (flip boundary, Sec.~8); related to, but not equal to, $\rho_{\mathrm{bw}}$.
\item $K_{\mathrm{eff}}=S_1^2/S_2$ --- excitation functional, with $S_1=\sum_j T_j$, $S_2=\sum_j T_j^2$.
\item $K_\infty=S_2/\|T\|_\infty^2$ --- Lindeberg spikiness functional (Gaussian/log-domain approximations).
\item $K_4=S_2^2/\sum_j T_j^4$ --- spectral-spread functional (permutation whitening, Sec.~7).
\item $C_0$ --- one-sample DGI correlator constant, $C_0=\mathbb{E}\|Z\|^2/S_2-1$; pipeline-specific, no universal value.
\item $D_H$ --- pairwise-Hadamard factor, $\approx 1$ for non-DC coefficients; measured per pipeline.
\item $B_L$ --- leverage of a linear reconstructor $L$, $B_L=(1/S_2)\sum_n b_n^2\|Le_n\|^2$ (Sec.~8).
\end{itemize}
\end{nameddef}

Pattern entries $m_n$ may be signed (Hadamard) or nonnegative (speckle) even though $T$ is nonnegative; where signed coefficients appear as carriers, the bucket conventions of Sec.~5 specify how they are physically realized.

\noindent\textbf{Algebraic identifiability.} Does the noiseless record determine $(a,T)$ up to gauge, or does some other pair $(a', T')$ with $\ell' - \ell \in S$ nonconstant reproduce the data exactly? This is a finite-sample, deterministic question about collisions $MT = D_s M T'$, and its answer depends on the design $M$ and on the dimension count $N$ versus $K + p$. Section~4 (Theorem~A and the tall-design thresholds, Theorem~A$'$) shows that every square invertible design --- however well-conditioned --- fails it, and that overdetermination restores it generically at $N \ge K + p$.

\noindent\textbf{Statistical identifiability.} When the patterns are drawn from a random ensemble, a weaker but operationally decisive property becomes available: if the carrier $B_n$ is \emph{stationary} --- its distribution does not depend on $n$ --- then any slow modulation of local averages over windows $W$ is attributable to the gain alone, and the relative gain is identifiable in distribution even where exact algebraic separation is impossible. This stationarity anchor is developed in Sec.~5 (Theorem~B) and is the mechanism the field's empirical successes have been implicitly exploiting.

\noindent\textbf{Estimation conditioning.} Uniqueness is not accuracy. Even when one of the first two notions holds, the finite-noise error of a concrete estimator is governed by conditioning quantities --- the window size $W$, the residual-gain variance $v$, the leverage $B_L$ of the reconstruction operator --- quantified by the rate analysis of Sec.~6 and the reconstruction bridge of Sec.~8. In particular, identifiability at the threshold $N = K + p$ says nothing about stability there.

We contend that much of the confusion surrounding blind gain correction stems from conflating these three notions: judging an acquisition scheme by the visual quality of a blindly corrected reconstruction entangles algebraic ambiguity, the presence or absence of a statistical anchor, and estimator conditioning into a single scalar, although the three failures call for entirely different remedies. Figure~1 disentangles them as a two-axis map --- inversion conditioning against blind identifiability --- on which ordered Hadamard, i.i.d.-random (DGI), tall random, and SRHT acquisitions occupy visibly different corners, a separation that Secs.~4 and 5 explain.

\section{Relation to prior work}

Gain--object separation is a bilinear inverse problem: writing the log-gain as $\ell_n=\log a_n$ and lifting the unknowns to the rank-one array $X=aT^{\top}$, each bucket reads $R_n=a_n B_n$ with $B_n=m_n^{\top}T$, a bilinear form in the object $T$ and the gain trace $a$. This places our question inside a mature literature on bilinear identifiability, and it is worth stating precisely what that literature settles and what it leaves open for the diagonal-gain geometry considered here.

The sharpest injectivity counts for lifted bilinear maps are Kech--Krahmer's optimal conditions for generic identifiability \cite{KechKrahmer1603.07316}, which for a rank-one measurement model require on the order of $2(n_1+n_2)-4$ generic measurements. These counts do not transfer to our setting: the operator $L_M(X)_n=a_n\,m_n^{\top}T$ is a \emph{diagonal-row} map, not a generic rank-one measurement, so the object carrier and the gain enter through a structured --- not generic --- set of linear forms. The relevant identifiability question is instead the intersection geometry of the object subspace with its gain-modulated copy, $\,U\cap D_sU$. Choudhary--Mitra's scaling laws for bilinear inverse problems \cite{ChoudharyMitra1402.2637} are the closest in spirit to our Theorem~A: their rank-two nullspace obstruction is exactly the mechanism by which a square unconstrained design absorbs a nonconstant gain into a redefined object. Li--Lee--Bresler's unified framework \cite{LiLeeBresler1501.06120} supplies the subspace- and sparsity-constrained identifiability thresholds that our tall-design counts ($N\ge K+p$ generic, $N\ge 2K+p-1$ uniform) specialize to for the diagonal-gain model. Ahmed--Recht--Romberg \cite{AhmedRechtRomberg1211.5608} establish convex recovery for blind deconvolution under subspace priors, and Kueng--Rauhut--Terstiege \cite{KuengRauhutTerstiege1410.6913} give the low-rank-from-rank-one-measurement guarantees; both are the algorithmic backdrop against which our purely algebraic and statistical criteria should be read.

On the imaging side, the practice of single-pixel and computational ghost imaging has long weighed ordered Hadamard bases against random speckle patterns for their conditioning and noise properties, but without an identifiability account of slow multiplicative drift. Learned correction of dynamic scaling factors in ghost imaging \cite{PengChen2024APL} and learned encoding for optical wireless links through complex channels \cite{PengXiaoChen2025OE} address the same physical nuisance empirically; our analysis explains why their \emph{static-correction ceiling} --- the error floor left by any time-independent correction (one learned or fitted on static data) when applied to a drifting record --- is the honest performance bound, and when a drifting gain becomes blindly separable at all. Blind self-calibration of unknown sensor gains and phases has a long history outside imaging --- from array-processing eigenstructure methods \cite{WeissFriedlander1990} to blind calibration of sensor networks \cite{Balzano2007IPSN} and biconvex compressive self-calibration \cite{LingStrohmer2015} --- and Balzano--Nowak's blind sensor calibration similarly finds gains recoverable under mild oversampling, which our tall-design threshold $N\ge K+p$ makes exact for the diagonal bilinear model. On the acquisition-design side, pink-noise speckle illumination \cite{Nie2021PRA} and sparse Hadamard sequencing \cite{Han2025OE} optimize the pattern set directly, but target additive-noise robustness, whereas our mechanism concerns multiplicative-drift identifiability.

Against this background the contribution of the present work is specific. We do not add to generic bilinear injectivity, which is settled by the references above. What is new is the temporal-statistical mechanism: relative-gain identifiability secured by making the object carrier stationary (Theorem~B), the associated nonparametric slow-gain calibration rate, the ordered-orthogonal confounding failure mode that this mechanism repairs, the SRHT synthesis that recovers orthogonal conditioning without sacrificing self-calibration, and the finite-noise reconstruction bridge that renders the phase diagram quantitative. The novelty is the stationarity anchor, not bilinear injectivity per se.

\section{The algebraic obstruction and when oversampling cures it}

The paradox of Sec.~1 is not an estimator artifact. Write the noiseless bucket record as $R_n = a_n B_n$ with $B_n = m_n^{\mathsf T}T$ the carrier coefficient of the object $T\in\mathbb R^{K}$ under illumination row $m_n$, and $\ell_n=\log a_n$ the log-gain. Stack $N=K$ frames into an invertible square design $M$. Then the multiplicative drift is absorbed \emph{exactly} into a relabelled object.

\begin{namedthm}{Theorem A (square non-identifiability)}
For any invertible $M\in\mathbb R^{K\times K}$ and any gain $a=(a_n)$ nonconstant on the support of the carrier $MT$, there exists a distinct object $T'\neq cT$ producing the identical bucket record: $\operatorname{diag}(a)MT = M T'$ with $T'=M^{-1}\operatorname{diag}(a)MT$. Gain--object separation is unidentifiable up to the global-scale gauge $a\mapsto ca,\ T\mapsto T/c$. (Proof: Appendix~A.)
\end{namedthm}

The object is a barcode printed onto time and the drift is a smudge across it; when the barcode carries slow structure, the theorem says the smudge can be \emph{reread as a different barcode} rather than dismissed as blur. This is a genuine orbit of the bilinear map, and it holds for \emph{every} invertible design --- Hadamard, Fourier, or otherwise. Two corollaries sharpen the trap.

\begin{namedthm}{Corollary 1 (slowness does not help)}
Restricting $\ell\in S$ to a slow drift class does not restore identifiability at $N=K$: for any nonconstant $s\in S$, the alternative pair $\big(\ell-s,\; T'=M^{-1}\operatorname{diag}(e^{s})MT\big)$ explains the record exactly, and the alternative log-gain $\ell-s$ is again an admissible slow drift because $S$ is a linear space. A slowly varying gain thus masquerades as a slowly varying object.
\end{namedthm}

\begin{namedthm}{Corollary 2 (positivity is not enough; known support zeros can be)}
Nonnegativity of $T$ alone does not break the orbit. \emph{Known} zeros in the object support \emph{can}: each enforced zero removes a degree of freedom from $T'$, and $K_0\ge p-1$ generic zeros at known support locations restore \emph{local} identifiability via a rank condition on the constrained collision map --- one per nonconstant drift dimension, $p-1=\dim(S/\mathrm{span}\{1\})$. This is a local rank criterion, not a global guarantee. (Proof: Appendix~A.)
\end{namedthm}

The first escape is overdetermination. Take a tall design $M\in\mathbb R^{N\times K}$, $N>K$, with $\ell\in S$, $\dim S=p\ge2$, $S\ni$ constants (the gauge). A collision $\operatorname{diag}(e^{s'})MT'=\operatorname{diag}(e^{s})MT$ pits $K+p$ unknowns against $N$ equations, and the naive dimension count suggests uniqueness once $N\ge K+p$; the content of the following theorem is that a parametric-transversality argument turns this count into proved sufficiency thresholds, for every fixed drift class $S\ni\mathbf 1$, at the price of almost-everywhere quantifiers whose exceptional sets are explicit Lebesgue-null sets.

\begin{namedthm}{Theorem A$'$ (proved tall-design identifiability results)}
Let $K\ge1$, $N\ge K$, and let $S\le\mathbb R^N$ be a fixed, known linear subspace of dimension $p\ge2$ with $\mathbf 1\in S$. For $M\in\mathbb R^{N\times K}$ define $\Phi_M(T,s)=D_{e^{s}}MT$ and $\mathcal U_M=\{T\in\mathbb R^K:(MT)_n\neq0\ \text{for every } n\}$. Parameters are identified modulo the positive global-scale gauge $(T,s)\sim(cT,\,s-\log c\,\mathbf 1)$, $c>0$.

\emph{(i) (Generic local threshold and genuine failure below it, $K\ge2$.)} There is a Lebesgue-null set $E_{\mathrm{loc}}\subset\mathbb R^{N\times K}\times\mathbb R^K$ such that for every $(M,T)\notin E_{\mathrm{loc}}$ with $T\in\mathcal U_M$ and every $s\in S$: $\operatorname{rank}D\Phi_M(T,s)=\min\{N,K+p-1\}$. Consequently $(T,s)$ is locally identifiable modulo gauge iff $N\ge K+p-1$. If $N\le K+p-2$ then a gauge-normalized neighborhood of $(T,s)$ contains a real-analytic manifold of exact aliases of dimension $K+p-N-1\ge1$, and every other sufficiently nearby point of it is gauge-inequivalent.

\emph{(ii) (Generic exact-identifiability sufficiency.)} If $N\ge K+p$, there is a Lebesgue-null set $E_{\mathrm{gen}}\subset\mathbb R^{N\times K}\times\mathbb R^K$ such that for every $(M,T)\notin E_{\mathrm{gen}}$ and every $s\in S$: $D_{e^{s'}}MT'=D_{e^{s}}MT$ with $(T',s')\in\mathbb R^K\times S$ implies $T'=cT$, $s'=s-\log c\,\mathbf 1$ for a unique $c>0$. Exact identifiability holds simultaneously for every possible true drift $s\in S$. This statement is \textbf{not} uniform over all objects for one fixed $M$.

\emph{(iii) (Uniform exact-identifiability sufficiency on the nonvanishing-carrier set.)} If $N\ge2K+p-1$, there is a Lebesgue-null set $E_{\mathrm{unif}}\subset\mathbb R^{N\times K}$ such that for every $M\notin E_{\mathrm{unif}}$, every $T\in\mathcal U_M$, every $s\in S$, and every $(T',s')\in\mathbb R^K\times S$: $D_{e^{s'}}MT'=D_{e^{s}}MT$ implies $T'=cT$, $s'=s-\log c\,\mathbf 1$ for a unique $c>0$. The same generic design works simultaneously for all nonvanishing-carrier objects and all true drifts in $S$.

\emph{(iv) (Degenerate one-dimensional object stratum.)} If $K=1$ then for every $M$ and every $T\in\mathcal U_M$ and every $s\in S$, the pair $(T,s)$ is globally identifiable modulo gauge, for every admissible $N$. Neither $N\ge K+p$ nor $N\ge2K+p-1$ is necessary when $K=1$.

(Proof: Appendix~B.)
\end{namedthm}

\noindent\textbf{Sharpness status.} Parts (ii) and (iii) are sufficient conditions, not proved if-and-only-if thresholds. For $K\ge2$, the below-wall part of (i) proves generic exact failure whenever $N\le K+p-2$. The remaining necessity assertion for the $K+p$ bound is the boundary case $N=K+p-1$: dimension counting predicts nonlocal aliases there, but dominance of the bad-pair projection has not been proved. Likewise, dimension counting predicts that a generic design fails uniform identifiability for some object when $N\le2K+p-2$, but this necessity direction has not been proved in the intermediate regime. Thus $K+p$ and $2K+p-1$ are conjecturally sharp for $K\ge2$ under generic/nondegenerate drift geometry, not universal sharp thresholds for every fixed $S$. The $K=1$ stratum explicitly rules out any universal necessity claim.

The proof is a parametric-transversality argument (Appendix~B): a rowwise perturbation of $M$ acts as a global witness on a collision incidence manifold, and no genericity of the drift family is invoked, so the statements hold verbatim for the physical Fourier low-pass class $S_{\mathrm{LP}}$ and for any other fixed drift convention containing the constants. Empirically, the $K=64$ and $K=128$ threshold scans of Sec.~9 (Fig.~8) show an exact $0\%\to100\%$ rank transition at $N=K+p-1$ across all five drift dimensions tested at both object sizes (3250 cells in total, no exceptions) --- a direct verification of the generic local rank wall of part (i). Throughout, part (ii) asserts \emph{generic exact finite-sample identifiability} --- uniqueness of $(a,T)$ up to gauge for almost every design--object pair --- not a conditioning bound and not an algorithmic recovery guarantee; stability near the thresholds is the separate third notion of Sec.~2, quantified in Secs.~6 and 8.

The picture is a dichotomy, and it sets up the rest of the paper. When $N\ge K+p$, overdetermination \emph{alone} separates gain from object for almost every design--object pair --- no statistical mechanism is logically needed, though randomization still buys stable conditioning (the identifiability axis of Fig.~1). When $K<N\le K+p-2$, generic local and exact identifiability fail through positive-dimensional nearby alias families; at $N=K+p-1$, local identifiability holds generically, while failure of global exact identifiability remains a dimension-count prediction. In that under-resourced window the stationarity anchor of Sec.~5 (or a support/sparsity prior) becomes \emph{required}, not merely convenient. The two regimes meet through the drift bandwidth: a gain occupying a fraction $\rho_{\mathrm{bw}}$ of the frame band spans $p\approx \rho_{\mathrm{bw}}N$ low-pass modes, so $N\ge K+p$ reads as

\[ N \;\gtrsim\; \frac{K}{1-\rho_{\mathrm{bw}}}. \]

Slow drift ($\rho_{\mathrm{bw}}\ll1$) is cured by modest oversampling; as $\rho_{\mathrm{bw}}\to1$, $p\to N$ and blind algebraic separation is impossible for \emph{any} $N$, forcing the statistical route.

\section{Statistical identifiability: the stationarity anchor}

Section~4 offered one escape from the gain--object orbit: buy identifiability with surplus rows. This section develops a second, logically distinct escape that operates even at fixed $N$ and, crucially, explains \emph{why} randomized acquisition self-calibrates while ordered acquisition does not. The idea is best stated before the mathematics. A drifting gain is a boat carried by a slow current, and the bucket record is our only view of the water. If the waves change character with position --- as the ordered Hadamard coefficient sequence does, sweeping through the object's spectral envelope --- we cannot tell the current from the coastline: a slow change in the record may be drift, or may be the scenery. But if the waves are \emph{statistically identical everywhere}, any slow modulation of the record has exactly one available explanation. Local averaging then drops an anchor into the distribution itself: it pins the \emph{relative} drift, while the absolute scale remains gauged away. This intuition, and nothing stronger, is what the results below formalize.

The log-domain record can be described by $Y_n=\log R_n=\ell_n+\log B_n$. The anchor requirement is a condition on the carrier alone:

\begin{nameddef}{Condition ($\star$) (stationary carrier, quantitative form)}
There is a scalar $m_T$ --- depending on the object but not on time, and absorbed by the global-scale gauge --- such that the windowed carrier averages over windows $W_n$ of length $W$ satisfy
\[
\begin{gathered}
\max_n \Big| W^{-1}\!\!\sum_{k\in W_n}\!\log B_k \;-\; m_T \Big| \;\longrightarrow\; 0\\
\text{in probability as } W\to\infty,\ W/N\to 0 .
\end{gathered}
\]
The condition presupposes a positive carrier on the analyzed record --- physically, a positivity/offset margin, or the soft-log $\psi_\alpha$ of Sec.~6 in the photon-counting case.
\end{nameddef}

The displayed criterion is deliberately \emph{uniform} --- a maximum over all window locations; pointwise consistency at a single $n$ is strictly weaker, and upgrading it to the displayed uniform control over the $\sim N$ window locations typically costs a $\log N$ / high-probability refinement (the $\delta\mapsto\delta/N$ step of Appendix~C).

I.i.d. random illumination satisfies ($\star$) by construction. For a finite random row permutation of a deterministic pattern set, or a single fixed SRHT draw, ($\star$) is \emph{not} automatic: a permuted record is exchangeable --- which anchors windowed averages in distribution --- but a particular draw can still fail for a spectrally concentrated object; the quantitative pixel-permutation version of this distinction is the permutation bound of Sec.~7 (pixel-level and acquisition-order randomization are distinct operations --- Appendix~E.6), and the permuted-arm rejections in Fig.~2 are its visible temporal-screen trace. For the i.i.d. case:

\begin{namedthm}{Proposition 1 (random-basis carrier)}
For i.i.d. random patterns with pixel mean $\mu_I$ and standard deviation $\sigma_I$, the carrier $\{B_n\}$ is white and strictly stationary, and the object enters its first two moments only through the scalars $S_1=\sum_j T_j$ and $S_2=\sum_j T_j^2$: $\mathbb{E}B_n=\mu_I S_1$, $\operatorname{Var}B_n=\sigma_I^2 S_2$, hence $\mathrm{CV}_B=(\sigma_I/\mu_I)/\sqrt{K_{\mathrm{eff}}}$ with $K_{\mathrm{eff}}=S_1^2/S_2$. Gaussian and log-domain approximations additionally require the Lindeberg-type spikiness condition $K_\infty=S_2/\|T\|_\infty^2\to\infty$; the reduction of the object to two scalars holds at this Gaussian-approximation level, not exactly. Higher cumulants and the log-domain mean can depend on the object's shape beyond $(S_1,S_2)$, but for an i.i.d. carrier every such term is time-independent and shifts only the gauge scalar $m_T$; it does not affect relative-gain recovery. (Proof: Appendix~C.)
\end{namedthm}

Figure~2 shows the two carriers side by side: the random trace is a flat, object-independent noise band, whereas the ordered Hadamard trace \emph{is} the object's coefficient envelope --- a deterministic, strongly non-stationary signature imprinted on acquisition time. Under ordered scanning the object masquerades as a temporal trend, precisely the degree of freedom a slow gain occupies, and ($\star$) fails. We emphasize the correct logical status of the condition:

\begin{namedthm}{Proposition 2 (($\star$) is an estimator criterion, not a universal one)}
Condition ($\star$) is necessary and sufficient for consistency of the blind windowed (stationarity-anchor) gain correction. It is \emph{not} an if-and-only-if condition for identifiability by all mechanisms: the overdetermined designs of Sec.~4 identify algebraically --- for almost every design--object pair at $N\ge K+p$, Theorem~A$'$(ii) --- without any stationarity, and conversely ($\star$) confers no exact algebraic identifiability. (Proof: Appendix~C.)
\end{namedthm}

\noindent\textbf{Bucket conventions.} Three bucket-signal conventions coexist in this paper, and the log-domain results apply to them differently. (i) \emph{Signed coefficient model:} $B_n$ is a signed transform coefficient (e.g.\ a bare Walsh--Hadamard coefficient); it can vanish or change sign, so $\log B_n$ is undefined and no log-domain gain estimator is available without further structure. (ii) \emph{Physical nonnegative offset model:} the optical bucket is a nonnegative intensity; signed patterns are realized with a bias/offset so that $B_n\ge B_{\min}>0$. Theorem~B and the estimators of Sec.~6 are stated in this model (assumption (iv) below). (iii) \emph{$\pm$ pairwise model:} each signed pattern is acquired as a complementary pair of nonnegative patterns whose buckets are differenced; the difference is signed again, but the drift now enters through adjacent-pair increments controlled by $\rho_{\mathrm{pair}}$ (Sec.~8). The raw signed arms of Sec.~9, which feed bare Walsh coefficients to the gain estimator, follow convention (i) and are \emph{diagnostic only}: they visualize the chronology failure but are not a valid physical log-domain estimator without the offset of (ii) or the pairing of (iii).

The main statistical result is then:

\begin{namedthm}{Theorem B (statistical relative-gain identifiability)}
Assume the log-domain model
\[Y_n=\log R_n=\ell_n+m_T+z_n,\]
where (i) $m_T=\mathbb{E}\log B_n$ is a scalar depending on the object but not on time; (ii) the carrier fluctuations $\{z_n\}$ are centered, strictly stationary, and weakly dependent with summable autocovariances, $\sigma_{\mathrm{abs}}^2=\sum_{h}|\gamma(h)|<\infty$ where $\gamma(h)=\operatorname{Cov}(z_0,z_h)$ ($\beta$-mixing with summable coefficients is sufficient) --- the \emph{signed long-run variance} $\sigma_{\mathrm{LR}}^2=\gamma(0)+2\sum_{h\ge1}\gamma(h)\ (\le\sigma_{\mathrm{abs}}^2)$ then also exists and is the asymptotic variance of window means; the two constants play distinct roles below; (iii) the log-gain $\ell\in S$ lies in a normalized-time H\"older-$\beta$ class, $|\ell_j-\ell_k|\le L\,(|j-k|/N)^{\beta}$; and (iv) $R_n>0$ on the analyzed record so the logarithm is defined (positivity/offset margin; the soft-log of Sec.~6 covers the photon-counting case). Then, for windows satisfying \textbf{both} $W\to\infty$ \textbf{and} $W/N\to 0$, windowed averages of $Y_n$ estimate $\ell_n+m_T$ consistently, with non-asymptotic stochastic error $O_P(\sigma_{\mathrm{abs}}/\sqrt{W})$ --- the asymptotic variance of the window mean being $\sigma_{\mathrm{LR}}^2/W$ --- and windowed drift bias $O(L\,(W/N)^{\beta})$; consequently the \emph{centered} log-gain $\ell_n-\overline{\ell}$ --- the relative gain, up to one global scale --- is identifiable and consistently estimable. The scalar $m_T$, and with it the absolute gauge of $\ell$, is not recoverable. (Proof: Appendix~C.)
\end{namedthm}

Theorem~B must be read with its scope intact. It is an asymptotic, high-probability statement about \emph{relative}-gain recovery up to the global-scale gauge --- never exact finite-sample identifiability of $(a,T)$. In particular it does not contradict Theorem~A: a random square design remains algebraically non-identifiable; what randomness buys is statistical separability, a different currency. This is also what distinguishes the mechanism from the bilinear-inverse identifiability literature \cite{LiLeeBresler1501.06120,KechKrahmer1603.07316,ChoudharyMitra1402.2637}: the anchor is a property of the \emph{temporal distribution} of the carrier, not of subspace injectivity.

The anchor also explains why the self-calibrating scaling-factor correction of Ref.~\cite{PengChen2024APL} can work at all. Under Proposition~1's Gaussian approximation the bucket record given $(a,\mu_B,\sigma_B)$ is a slow trend plus stationary residuals, and an SCGI-style Gaussian-likelihood fit of that trend is a maximum-likelihood realization of the windowed anchor estimator, inheriting Theorem~B's consistency guarantee (and its gauge).

Figure~3 is the core numerical evidence. Across a panel of objects, the blind gain-estimation error $\|\hat a-a\|/\|a\|$ under random illumination tracks $\mathrm{CV}_B/\sqrt{W}$ object by object --- to leading Gaussian/log-variance order, and within the tested object panel, the object enters only through $K_{\mathrm{eff}}$ --- and once normalized by that floor the curves collapse across objects to within $\pm5$--$7\%$; under ordered Hadamard the curves scatter by object and do not decay with $W$ --- the object envelope is a bias no amount of averaging removes. The window $W$ itself carries a bias--variance trade-off (drift curvature versus $\mathrm{CV}_B^2/W$), whose optimal resolution and minimax rate are the subject of Sec.~6.

\section{Estimation rate, lower bounds, and low-photon robustness}

Section~5 establishes that relative gain is \emph{identifiable} from a stationary carrier; here we show it is recovered \emph{efficiently and predictably}. The baseline estimator is the plain windowed log of Theorem~B: average $Y_n=\log R_n$ over a window $W$ of frames sharing a slowly varying gain. For photon-counting records we work with its robust generalization, the windowed \emph{soft-log}: average $\psi_\alpha(c)=\log(c+\alpha)$ and invert the calibration curve, $\hat\theta_n=m_\alpha^{-1}\!\big(\mathrm{mean}_W\,\psi_\alpha\big)$, where $m_\alpha(\theta)=\mathbb{E}[\psi_\alpha(C)]$ denotes the calibration curve for $C\sim\mathrm{Pois}(\Lambda_0 e^{\theta} B+d)$. As $\alpha\to0$ at high mean count $\bar\lambda$ the calibration curve becomes the identity and the soft-log reduces to the plain windowed log estimator; the rate theorem below is stated for the general (soft-log) estimator and covers both regimes. Because the log-gain $\ell$ lies in a H\"older-$\beta$ class of smooth drift (notation warning: $\beta$ is reserved for H\"older smoothness and $\alpha$ for the soft-log offset throughout; the two symbols are unrelated and must not be conflated), the window trades bias against variance in the usual nonparametric way: wider $W$ suppresses variance as $O(1/W)$ but incurs an approximation bias set by the local smoothness $\beta$ and the drift Lipschitz constant $L_a$ (the per-frame form of Theorem~B's normalized-time constant, $L_a=L\,N^{-\beta}$).

\begin{namedthm}{Theorem C (windowed rate)}
For log-gain in a H\"older-$\beta$ class with constant $L_a$, the bias-optimized windowed estimator attains
\[\mathrm{MSE}^{*}\;\le\; C\,\kappa_{\min}^{-2}\,\big[\kappa_{\max}L_a\big]^{2/(2\beta+1)}\,\sigma_{\alpha,\mathrm{LR}}^{4\beta/(2\beta+1)},\]
the standard nonparametric rate in the effective per-frame noise $\sigma_{\alpha,\mathrm{LR}}$. (Proof: Appendix~D.)
\end{namedthm}

This rate is not merely achievable but \emph{optimal}. An Assouad two-point construction over the same smoothness class (a van Trees / Pinsker argument in the Sobolev variant) produces a matching lower bound, so no estimator improves on Theorem~C by more than constants.

\begin{namedthm}{Theorem D (minimax optimality)}
Over the H\"older-$\beta$ drift class the windowed estimator is minimax-rate optimal: the lower bound matches Theorem~C at the level of rates. Sharp minimax constants remain open. (Proof: Appendix~D.)
\end{namedthm}

The floor beneath both bounds is Fisher-information geometry. Working in the gauge quotient (global scale is unobservable, Sec.~5), the relevant object is the \emph{quotient Fisher information}: the Fisher matrix restricted to directions transverse to the scale orbit. This makes the identifiability dichotomy quantitative --- the Fisher matrix is singular exactly along the null direction of Theorem~A. Non-identifiability is not poor conditioning; it is a genuine flat direction of the likelihood, the ambiguity $(a,T)\mapsto(a s^{-1}, sT)$ made infinitesimal.

Which design constant controls what is a spikiness question, and three distinct functionals appear. $K_{\mathrm{eff}}=S_1^2/S_2$ (Prop~1) governs excitation and the pairwise-Hadamard residual-gain budget; $K_\infty=S_2/\|T\|_\infty^2$ is the Lindeberg spikiness parameter gating the Gaussian and log-domain approximations (Prop~1) and the windowed-energy concentration; $K_4=S_2^2/\sum_j T_j^4$ enters the concentration guarantee for permutation whitening (Sec.~7, via $\min(\epsilon^2 K_4,\epsilon K_\infty)\ge C\log(K/\delta)$). Conflating them mislabels the operative bottleneck: a design flat in $K_{\mathrm{eff}}$ can still be spiky in $K_\infty$.

Low photon counts do not break the log domain. The Poisson derivative identity $\frac{d}{d\lambda}\mathbb{E}f(C)=\mathbb{E}[f(C{+}1){-}f(C)]$ gives a sensitivity $\kappa_\alpha(\lambda)\to 1$ at high $\bar\lambda$ and $\kappa_\alpha(\lambda)\to\lambda\log(1+1/\alpha)$ as $\bar\lambda\to 0$; the offset $\alpha$ absorbs empty bins so no frame ever contributes $\log 0$. Propagating through Theorem~C, the photon-starved variance degrades gracefully as $\sim 1/(W\bar\lambda)$ --- the Fisher-matched scaling, since the Poisson information for log-intensity is $\lambda$ itself. Zero photons carry zero Fisher information; they cut precision but never diverge (Fig.~7).

The caveat is stated honestly, and the calibration inversion is what resolves it. Without $m_\alpha^{-1}$, a plain windowed average of $\psi_\alpha$ --- the \emph{uncalibrated proxy} --- becomes shrinkage-bias-dominated at $\bar\lambda\lesssim1$: $\psi_\alpha$ pulls the estimate toward $\log\alpha$, and the proxy's MSE sits \emph{below} its unbiased (local) Fisher reference not because it beats the bound but because bias is present. The calibrated estimator of Theorem~C removes this shrinkage and tracks the $1/(W\bar\lambda)$ law throughout the variance-limited regime without ever falling below the Fisher reference; what it gives up is precisely the bias-bought low-count MSE discount of the proxy (and of the Anscombe transform) --- the honest accounting anticipated here and quantified in Sec.~9 (Fig.~7).

\section{Basis design: randomized orthogonal acquisition (SRHT)}

A reader who has followed the stationarity anchor of Sec.~5 will raise a natural objection: the anchor was purchased with random illumination, yet the appeal of ordered Hadamard was its exact, perfectly conditioned inverse. Must one surrender orthogonal conditioning to buy self-calibration? The answer is no. The randomized Hadamard transform --- an orthogonal Hadamard carrier composed with a random sign flip and a random pixel permutation, known in the sketching literature as the SRHT (subsampled randomized Hadamard transform) --- keeps a full-rank, well-conditioned forward map while randomizing the \emph{temporal handwriting} of the object. One shuffles the deck without bending the cards. We use the \emph{full} $N=K$ randomized transform throughout; ``subsampled'' is reserved for the $N<K$ case, which additionally raises a bias/prior question outside our scope.

The mechanism is exact. Writing the per-coefficient excitation as $Z_g$ under a random diagonal sign $D$, the residual gain--object coupling is governed entirely by the Walsh spectrum of the squared object. With $w = T^2$,
\[\operatorname{Cov}_D(Z_g, Z_h) = \hat{w}(g+h),\]
the Walsh transform of $w$ evaluated at the dyadic sum of indices. Sign randomization alone therefore whitens the excitation --- decorrelates every coefficient pair --- \textbf{if and only if} $w$ is Walsh-flat, i.e.\ $\hat{w}(k)$ is negligible for all $k\neq 0$. This is a condition on the object, not the design: a structured $T$ concentrates $\hat{w}$ off the origin and sign flips cannot remove it.

The random permutation supplies the missing genericity. It acts as a probabilistic Walsh-flattener: permuting pixels spreads the mass of $\hat{w}$ across the spectrum, and the off-origin covariance is driven below tolerance $\varepsilon$ with probability $1-\delta$ once the object is spectrally spread enough,
\[\min\!\big(\varepsilon^2 K_4,\ \varepsilon K_\infty\big) \ \geq\ C \log(K/\delta),\]
with $K_4$ and $K_\infty$ the spread functionals of Sec.~6. Both diverge as the object's energy delocalizes and collapse when a few pixels dominate; the bound quantifies precisely how much delocalization the permutation needs.

Permutation is a genuine second ingredient, not a substitute for the analysis. Under permutation alone,
\[\operatorname{Var}_P Z_g = \frac{K S_2 - S_1^2}{K-1} = S_2\,\frac{K - K_{\mathrm{eff}}}{K-1},\]
so the residual carries an $O(1/K_{\mathrm{eff}})$ \emph{upper} bound on variance --- reassuring on average --- but no matching lower bound. A flat object realizes the gap: it produces zero excitation, so permutation cannot guarantee two-sided control coefficient by coefficient. The whitening claim is one-sided unless the sign flip and the spectral-spread condition are both in force. A row-order shuffle of the acquisition sequence is a third, weaker operation: it makes the temporal carrier exchangeable --- enough for the windowed anchor of Sec.~5 to hold in distribution --- but it does not by itself guarantee two-sided per-coefficient excitation. Figure~6 ablates the acquisition-time operations (none / row-order permutation / sign / both); its permutation arms reorder acquisition time ($P_{\mathrm{row}}$) and do \emph{not} realize the pixel permutation $P_{\mathrm{col}}$ of the whitening analysis above, whose factorized attribution --- including the $P_{\mathrm{col}}$-only and $P_{\mathrm{col}}{+}D$ arms in natural acquisition order --- is reported in Sec.~9.6 and Appendix~E.6.

This yields a design rule rather than a single number.

\begin{nameddef}{Design rule (blind slow gain)}
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*),leftmargin=*]
\item Do \emph{not} acquire in ordered Hadamard when the nuisance is a blind, slowly drifting gain --- the ordering hands the object a temporal signature (Sec.~4).
\item Randomize the carrier: apply the SRHT sign flip \emph{and} permutation.
\item Verify Walsh-flatness of the realized (permuted) $T^2$ --- the covariance-whitening guarantee is the sign-randomization statement applied after the permutation --- or rely on the permutation bound above with $K_4, K_\infty$ estimated from the object class.
\item Keep a positivity / offset margin so the log or soft-log stage of Sec.~6 stays away from its singularity.
\item Choose the averaging window $W$ from the bias--variance trade of Sec.~6, not from conditioning.
\end{enumerate}
\end{nameddef}

The rule prescribes \emph{both} randomizations because that is the configuration with a two-sided theorem guarantee under the stated spectral-spread condition. The ablation of Fig.~6 reports a different, empirical fact: in the tested panel, either randomization alone already recovered essentially the full advantage. The two statements must not be merged --- the theorem guarantees whitening under its hypotheses, while the ablation shows that, for these objects and this drift, any tested way of breaking the chronology sufficed; we do not promote the panel result to a universal claim.

The synthesis is that orthogonality and self-calibration are compatible: SRHT preserves the $B_L = 1$ leverage of a full orthogonal inversion (Sec.~8) while --- under Walsh-flatness of $T^2$ or a successful permutation of a sufficiently spread object --- making the object carrier statistically stationary in time, so that the only non-stationary structure left in the bucket record is the gain itself. The conditioning that made ordered Hadamard attractive is retained; only its chronology is discarded.

(Proof of the Walsh identity and permutation bound: Appendix~E.)

\section{From identifiability to images: the reconstruction bridge and phase diagram}

Sections~4--7 answer the question a statistician asks --- \emph{is the gain identifiable, and at what rate?} --- but an imaging practitioner asks a different one: \emph{how many gray levels do I lose?} This section supplies the bridge. The result is exact, holds at finite $N$ and finite photon count, and reduces every acquisition strategy in this paper to three numbers that can be read off a single chart before any hardware is built.

The corrected bucket record can be described by $z_n=(1+\delta_n)b_n+\xi_n$ with $b=AT$, where $\delta_n$ denotes the \emph{residual} multiplicative gain after whatever blind correction the pipeline applied; let $\hat T=Lz$ be any linear reconstruction.

\begin{namedthm}{Theorem 1 (master finite-noise relMSE identity)}
Assume --- as part of the hypothesis, not as background convention --- that (H1) $\mathbb{E}\,\xi=0$; (H2) the residual gain $\delta$ and the additive noise $\xi$ are uncorrelated, $C_{\delta\xi}:=\operatorname{Cov}(\delta,\xi)=0$; (H3) the gain estimate $\hat a$ is \emph{exogenous} with respect to the reconstructed record --- computed independently of the noise realization $\xi$ (injected or synthetic residuals, an independent calibration record, or a cross-fitted estimate from held-out frames), so that (H1)--(H2) hold conditionally on the record being reconstructed; and (H4) the second moments $m_\delta$, $V_\delta$, $\Sigma_\xi$ are finite. Then, without Gaussian or small-noise approximation,
\begin{align*}
\frac{\mathbb{E}\|\hat T-T\|^2}{S_2}\;=\;&\underbrace{\frac{\|(LA-I)T+L\,\mathrm{diag}(b)\,m_\delta\|^2}{S_2}}_{\text{bias}}\\
&+\;\underbrace{\frac{\operatorname{tr}\!\big(L\,\mathrm{diag}(b)\,V_\delta\,\mathrm{diag}(b)\,L^\top\big)}{S_2}}_{\text{residual gain}}\\
&+\;\underbrace{\frac{\operatorname{tr}\!\big(L\,\Sigma_\xi L^\top\big)}{S_2}}_{\text{read + Poisson}}.
\end{align*}
If the residual gains are independent with $\mathrm{Var}\,\delta_n=v$, the gain term collapses to $v\,B_L$ with the \textbf{leverage} $B_L=(1/S_2)\sum_n b_n^2\|Le_n\|^2$; coherent (smooth) residuals require the full matrix form. (Proof: Appendix~F.)
\end{namedthm}

The hypotheses are not decorative. A gain estimate computed from the \emph{same} noisy record that carries $\xi$ --- the generic situation for a blind pipeline --- generically violates (H1)--(H3): the mean of $\hat T-T$ acquires an additional $L\,m_\xi$ bias term inside the bias norm, and the covariance acquires the cross-term $(2/S_2)\,\operatorname{tr}\!\big[L\,\mathrm{diag}(b)\,C_{\delta\xi}\,L^{\top}\big]$, neither of which is included above (Remark~F.1.1). The exact identity is accordingly validated in the injected-residual (exogenous) regime --- experiment E4 of Sec.~9 --- and applies to cross-fitted estimates that restore (H1)--(H2) by construction; for same-record blind pipelines it is exact only up to those two extra terms.

The Poisson plug-in $\Sigma_\xi=\mathrm{diag}(\tau_{G,n}^2+b_n^2/\Phi_n)$ is exact at all photon counts \emph{after the post-calibration substitution $\hat a_n\to a_n$}; before that substitution both noise variances carry the factor $(a_n/\hat a_n)^2$ (Appendix~F). With that qualifier, the identity --- not an approximation to it --- is what Fig.~4 tests. One metric convention matters when reading measured numbers: the identity is stated for the raw error $\mathbb{E}\|\hat T-T\|^2/S_2$, whereas the relMSE \emph{measured} in Figs.~4--5 is computed after least-squares scale alignment of $\hat T$ to $T$ (the gauge convention of Sec.~2); alignment can pull $\mathrm{relMSE}/v$ below 1 for DC-dominated objects by absorbing the gain component co-linear with the object (Sec.~9). The two roles are kept separate in what follows: the \emph{raw} (un-aligned) identity is verified directly by the injected coherent-residual experiment of Sec.~9 (E4, Supplementary Material), while Fig.~4 reads the scale-aligned image metric. Specializing $L$:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Orthogonal / SRHT full inversion:} $B_L=1$, so $\mathrm{relMSE}=v+(1/S_2)\sum_n(\tau_{G,n}^2+b_n^2/\Phi_n)$. The imager passes residual gain through at unit leverage --- no amplification, no dilution.
\item \textbf{Pairwise (differenced) Hadamard:} the gain term is $R_{\mathrm{pair}} = (K_{\mathrm{eff}}/4)\,D_H(T)\,\mathrm{Var}(\Delta)$, with $D_H\approx 1$ for non-DC coefficients and $\mathrm{Var}(\Delta)\approx 2s^2\,r(\rho_{\mathrm{pair}})$ the adjacent-pair gain increment, where $r(\cdot)$ denotes the adjacent-pair decorrelation function of the drift --- $r(\rho)=1-e^{-\rho}$ for the OU drift of Sec.~9, with $r(\rho)\approx\rho$ for small $\rho$. Differencing converts \emph{slowness} of the drift directly into suppression.
\item \textbf{Random / DGI:} $\mathrm{relMSE}_{\mathrm{DGI}} = C_0(\mathrm{pipeline})/N + (1+C_0^{\mathrm{lev}})v/N + $ noise terms, where $C_0(\mathrm{pipeline})=\mathbb{E}\|Z\|^2/S_2-1$ denotes the exact one-sample sampling-floor constant of the correlator ($C_0=K+\beta_4-2$ for symmetric zero-mean patterns) and $C_0^{\mathrm{lev}}$ the \emph{drift-leverage} constant of the same pipeline: mean subtraction removes the $K_{\mathrm{eff}}K(\mu/\sigma)^2$ background penalty from the sampling floor but \textbf{not} from the drift leverage --- a time-varying gain modulates the large DC background coherently --- so for mean-subtracted pipelines $C_0^{\mathrm{lev}}$ equals the raw-correlator constant $C_0^{\mathrm{raw}}$ of (F.10), roughly $10^3\times$ the pipeline $C_0$ at nonnegative-background operating points (Appendix~F.3.3). There is \textbf{no universal $C_0$} --- hence the argument: it depends on the pattern statistics, background, and normalization, and must be measured from the pipeline at hand --- for our SCGI-style testbed \cite{PengChen2024APL} the measured values are used throughout Figs.~4 and 5.
\end{itemize}

This yields an honest corollary that the identifiability results alone would obscure: \textbf{at equal residual variance $v$, orthogonal inversion is the best conduit} --- it has $B_L=1$ and no $C_0(\mathrm{pipeline})/N$ estimator floor. The randomized bases do not win because they transmit gain error more gracefully; they win because Theorem~B and the window estimator of Sec.~6 let $v$ \emph{itself} be driven down blindly, at rate $1/W$ per window, whereas an ordered scan offers no blind handle on $v$ at all.

The competition between these closed forms produces a finite-$N$ flip boundary.

\begin{namedthm}{Prop 3 (finite-$N$ flip boundary)}
$\rho^{*}=\inf\{\rho_{\mathrm{pair}}\ge0: R_{\mathrm{pair}}(\rho_{\mathrm{pair}})\ge R_{\mathrm{rand}}(\rho_{\mathrm{pair}},N)\}$ satisfies, in the small-drift regime and under the single-crossing assumption of Appendix~F.4,
\[
\begin{aligned}
r(\rho^{*})=\frac{2}{K_{\mathrm{eff}}\,D_H\,s^{2}}\big[&C_0(\mathrm{pipeline})/N\\
&+(1+C_0^{\mathrm{lev}})\,v_{\mathrm{blind}}(\rho^{*},N)/N\\
&+R_{\mathrm{DGI,noise}}-R_{\mathrm{pair,noise}}\big],
\end{aligned}
\]
where $C_0^{\mathrm{lev}}$ is the pipeline's drift-leverage constant ($C_0^{\mathrm{lev}}=C_0^{\mathrm{raw}}$ for mean-subtracted DGI pipelines; $C_0^{\mathrm{lev}}=C_0$ only without background subtraction --- Appendix~F.3.3), and whose leading order ($r(\rho)=\rho$, $D_H=1$, negligible noise and $v_{\mathrm{blind}}$) is the heuristic $\rho^{*}\approx 2C_0(\mathrm{pipeline})/(N K_{\mathrm{eff}} s^{2})$. For mean-subtracted pipelines ``negligible $v_{\mathrm{blind}}$'' means $(1+C_0^{\mathrm{raw}})v_{\mathrm{blind}}\ll C_0$, a far stronger requirement than $v_{\mathrm{blind}}\ll C_0$. (Proof: Appendix~F.)
\end{namedthm}

\begin{nameddef}{Remark (two decorrelation conventions --- do not conflate)}
Throughout, $\rho_{\mathrm{pair}}$ denotes \emph{adjacent-pair} gain decorrelation, the variable entering $\mathrm{Var}(\Delta)$ and Prop~3; $\rho_{\mathrm{bw}}$ denotes the normalized gain \emph{bandwidth}, $p\approx\rho_{\mathrm{bw}}N$, the variable entering the tall-design thresholds of Sec.~4 ($N\gtrsim K/(1-\rho_{\mathrm{bw}})$). Both grow with drift speed but are not equal, and every axis in Fig.~5 is labeled by which one it carries.
\end{nameddef}

Figure~4 shows the bridge validated term by term: predicted bias, gain, and noise contributions against measured relMSE across bases, drift speeds, and photon budgets --- numerical evidence, on simulation, that Theorem~1 is an accounting identity rather than a bound. Figure~5 reports the equal-frame competition on the $(\rho_{\mathrm{pair}},\sigma_a)$ grid: the empirical winner map, the SRHT-paired-versus-ordered-Hadamard crossover, and the no-free-parameter skeleton test of Prop~3 on a gain-known random arm (Sec.~9.4); the crossings drawn in Fig.~5 are the empirical SRHT-paired crossover, a comparison Prop~3 does not rank, while for the blind random+DGI arm the corrected leverage constant predicts --- and the data confirm --- no flip anywhere on this grid. Three regimes emerge. For \textbf{slow drift} ($\rho_{\mathrm{pair}}<\rho^{*}$), pairwise Hadamard is fine: differencing suppresses what little the gain moves, and orthogonal conditioning is kept for free. For \textbf{intermediate drift}, randomized carriers win --- the stationarity anchor buys a small $v_{\mathrm{blind}}$ that no ordered scan can reach; the above-floor winner is the SRHT-paired pipeline, which keeps orthogonal conditioning and avoids the $C_0(\mathrm{pipeline})/N$ estimator floor that bare i.i.d.-random/DGI pays in absolute error. For \textbf{fast drift} ($\rho_{\mathrm{bw}}\to 1$, $p$ approaching $N$), every \emph{blind algebraic} strategy hits the noise floor together --- consistent with the information-loss intuition behind Theorem~A$'$'s counting; this is a statement about blind separation, not about the physics --- an externally calibrated or metered gain restores the problem to ordinary inversion. The chart is a pre-acquisition instrument: locate your drift spectrum and photon budget on it, and the go/no-go decision --- and the basis --- follow before the first frame is taken.

\section{Numerical experiments}

The theory of Secs.~4--8 makes quantitative predictions --- a stationarity dichotomy, a $W^{-1/2}$ estimation law whose object dependence is confined to $K_{\mathrm{eff}}$, an exact relMSE identity, a flip boundary, and a photon-limited Fisher scaling --- and this section tests each of them in a series of simulations. A closing subsection integrates five verification experiments completed after the main panel: the tall-design rank threshold (Fig.~8), permutation-whitening power, the coherent-residual validation of Theorem~1, the $C_0$ audit, and oracle-to-blind baselines (Supplementary Material S1--S4, assembled with the submission package).

\noindent\textbf{Protocol.} All results are simulations; no hardware data enter, consistent with the scope of Sec.~1. Comparisons are made under matched acquisition budgets --- equal frame counts and equal total flux for every arm --- so that no basis is granted extra photons or extra rows. The drift is an Ornstein--Uhlenbeck log-gain trace with per-frame decorrelation rate $\rho$, the knob that sets the adjacent-pair decorrelation $\rho_{\mathrm{pair}}$ of Sec.~8; within each seed a single trace is shared by every measurement arm, so the bases face the identical nuisance realization rather than their own luck, while the traces differ across seeds (drift standard deviation 0.030--0.104). The H\"older-$\beta$ deviation model of Theorems~B--C is the \emph{analysis frame}, and the OU trace is the \emph{simulation protocol}: an OU/AR(1) path does not literally satisfy a $\beta=1$ Lipschitz deviation bound (its increments are rough at every scale), and the windowed estimator is applied to it without any smoothness-based tuning. Blind correction uses a single estimator for all arms --- the sliding-window mean automatic gain control (AGC) realization of the proposed windowed estimator of Sec.~6 --- with no per-arm tuning and no denoising or post-processing anywhere in the pipeline. The object panel holds ten objects spanning $K_{\mathrm{eff}}=S_1^2/S_2$ from 60 to 864, each run at five drift seeds (fifteen for the reconstruction bridge), with errors computed against ground truth on the object support mask after the least-squares scale alignment of Sec.~8. Concretely: the object panel comprises ten shared objects from a fixed generator --- five synthetic MNIST-style digits, the letters A and L, a periodic vertical stripe pattern (stripe width $\approx$ image/8), a ring (radii $\approx$ 0.22/0.34 of the image span), and one procedural natural patch --- each normalized to $[0,1]$ and spanning $K_{\mathrm{eff}}=59.8$--$864.3$; every experiment below shares base seed $20240708$. Per-figure enumerables:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Fig.~2 (carrier stationarity):} 8192 frames per arm at $64\times64$, split into 8 chunks with a 128-frame transient cutoff, AGC window $W=64$, five arms.
\item \textbf{Fig.~3 (blind gain identifiability):} $32\times32$ objects ($K=1024$), 2048 frames per arm, nine arms --- the raw signed-Walsh arms at both the frame-matched 2048 budget (sequence acquired twice) and the single-pass 1024 diagnostic --- $\rho_{\mathrm{pair}}\in\{10^{-3},10^{-2}\}$, drift scale $s=0.1$, $W$ swept over a log-spaced grid from 4 to 1024; every row is scored under both the ratio AGC and the log-domain estimator of Theorem~B; the final uncertainty replication (Sec.~9.2, Table~1) uses 20 drift seeds per (object, $\rho$) pair --- 31{,}600 rows --- while the $R^2$ exploration above used 5 seeds.
\item \textbf{Fig.~4 (reconstruction bridge):} injected $v\in\{0,10^{-4},3\times10^{-4},10^{-3},3\times10^{-3},10^{-2}\}$, $N\in\{1024,2048,4096\}$, 15 seeds, three reconstruction arms.
\item \textbf{Fig.~7 (low-photon robustness):} mean photon budgets $\bar\lambda\in\{0.25,\dots,128\}$ on a $\times2$ grid, soft-log $\alpha=0.5$, $W=64$, $\rho_{\mathrm{pair}}=10^{-3}$, $s=0.1$, 5 seeds, with four estimator arms --- naive clipped log, Anscombe, the uncalibrated soft-log proxy, and the calibrated soft-log of Theorem~C ($m_\alpha^{-1}$ tabulated exactly by truncated Poisson summation, tail bound $\le3.5\times10^{-34}$) --- compared on the same paired Poisson draw at each budget.
\item \textbf{Fig.~8 + Supplementary Material (threshold, whitening power, coherent residuals, $C_0$, baselines):} rank/solver threshold scan at $K=64$ (20 seeds, 1300 cells) and replicated at $K=128$ (30 seeds, 1950 cells), $p\in\{3,5,9,17,33\}$, margins $N-K-p\in[-8,+16]$ (lower end truncated at $N>K$ for small $p$); whitening power with 32 permutation draws $\times$ 10 objects under the Fig.~2 protocol (the historical arm applies a pixel permutation $P_{\mathrm{col}}$ \emph{and} an independent acquisition-order permutation $P_{\mathrm{row}}$; a factorized ablation separates $P_{\mathrm{col}}$, $D$, and $P_{\mathrm{row}}$ at 32 draws per arm --- Appendix~E.6); E4 coherent-residual injection with five covariance structures at $v=10^{-3}$, $K=N=1024$; $C_0$ audit with four pattern ensembles $\times$ five objects, $2\times10^{4}$ single-frame draws each; oracle/metered/blind bars on four objects $\times$ five gain treatments $\times$ five seeds.
\item \textbf{Pattern conventions:} i.i.d.\ uniform $[0,1]$; i.i.d.\ binary Bernoulli(0.5); paired Hadamard as complementary $(1\pm H)/2$ frames (2 frames per coefficient); SRHT as $H\cdot\mathrm{diag}(\pm1)$ with an optional row permutation, paired the same way; raw signed Walsh arms are diagnostic-only (Sec.~5 bucket convention (i)) and not a valid physical estimator without offset or pairing.
\item \textbf{Metrics:} blind gain error is the scale-aligned relative error (both the estimated and true gain traces normalized to unit mean, with the optimal scalar alignment of Sec.~8); mask-based contrast uses GT-mask CNR at threshold 0.5; reconstruction error is the scale-aligned relMSE of Sec.~8. The OU drift trace is generated with \texttt{normalize\_mean=1}.

\noindent\textbf{Carrier stationarity (Fig.~2).} This experiment applies a \emph{variance-stationarity diagnostic} to the noiseless carrier $\{B_n\}$, before any gain is applied, at 8192 frames per arm on $64\times64$ objects; the Brown--Forsythe test checks chunk-variance equality --- a consequence of the stationarity that ($\star$) requires, not a direct test of ($\star$)'s windowed log-carrier means. As shown in Fig.~2, the Brown--Forsythe variance-stationarity test at $p<10^{-3}$ rejects 7 of 10 objects for ordered Hadamard, with $p$ reaching $10^{-20}$, against 0 of 10 for both i.i.d.\ random ensembles. (These are uncorrected per-test $p$-values; under a multiplicity correction across the object $\times$ arm panel --- e.g.\ Bonferroni --- the strongest rejections clear the adjusted threshold by many orders of magnitude, while rejections near the $p<10^{-3}$ gate should be read with that correction in mind.) The permuted arms --- randomly permuted paired Hadamard, an acquisition-order reordering $P_{\mathrm{row}}$, and SRHT, which adds pixel signs $D$ (Appendix~E.6) --- each reject 2 of 10: a \emph{fixed} reordering draw does not guarantee temporal homogeneity for every object --- the randomization guarantees of Secs.~5 and 7 are probabilistic statements over draws, and an object with concentrated spectral energy can alias against a particular draw. This residue is the theory's own prediction, not an anomaly. The pixel-level randomization of the SRHT analysis ($P_{\mathrm{col}}$, $D$) is probed separately by the factorized ablation of Sec.~9.6. The Parseval invariant closes the loop: every exhaustively paired arm has carrier $\mathrm{CV}_B = 1/\sqrt{K_{\mathrm{eff}}}$ to a relative accuracy of about $10^{-6}$, and the random arms match $(\sigma_I/\mu_I)/\sqrt{K_{\mathrm{eff}}}$ within $\pm2\%$ --- so the ordered pathology lives entirely in the temporal variance structure, not in the marginal spread of the carrier. The rejection proportions are descriptive counts over this ten-object panel (no resampled intervals).

\noindent\textbf{Blind gain identifiability (Fig.~3).} This is the core evidence for Theorem~B, run over nine arms including the raw, non-paired Hadamard arms that feed bare signed Walsh coefficients to the estimator --- convention (i) of the bucket conventions of Sec.~5, a \emph{diagnostic-only} configuration that is not a valid physical log-domain estimator without offset or pairing. The raw signed-Walsh arms are scored at the same 2048-frame budget as the physical arms (the 1024-pattern sequence acquired twice against the same drift trace); the single-pass 1024-frame variants are retained as diagnostics and are indistinguishable (median best-window error $0.896$ versus $0.899$ ordered, $0.788$ versus $0.794$ shuffled), so the raw-arm failure is a chronology effect, not a budget effect. As shown in Fig.~3, the chronology failure of Theorem~A is made visible: under the ratio AGC the raw ordered arm's blind gain error is $0.94$--$0.99$ across all averaging windows (median best-window $0.899$ $[0.892,0.903]$, still $0.950$ at $W=512$) and does not decay. Shuffling the raw frames improves this to $0.794$ $[0.775,0.816]$ --- permutation helps even here --- but the arm remains far above the random and paired arms at $0.007$--$0.017$, because without pairing the near-zero-mean carrier leaves the AGC ill-posed. Every arm is additionally scored under the log-domain estimator faithful to Theorem~B (windowed means of $\log R$, masked to the $R>0$ record per assumption (iv)): the two estimators agree to within about $2\%$ on all stationary-carrier arms and give the same qualitative ordering, and under the log estimator the raw ordered arm still cannot fall below $0.71$ (median $0.708$ $[0.563,0.840]$, non-decaying) while raw shuffled improves to $0.136$ $[0.083,0.196]$ --- still at least $12\times$ above every randomized arm. Within the variance-obeying arms, the slow-drift variance-segment slopes of $\log(\mathrm{err})$ against $\log W$ are $-0.39$ (random binary), $-0.60$ (SRHT), and $-0.61$ (randomly permuted paired Hadamard) --- all inside the $W^{-1/2}$ band --- while naturally ordered paired Hadamard is shallow at $-0.24$: differencing cancels the drift within pairs but leaves the ordered envelope. Normalizing by the theory floor $\sqrt{\mathrm{CV}_B^2/W}$ collapses the panel: the paired arms sit at $0.40$--$0.41$ and the random arms at $0.89$--$0.90$, each bundle collapsing across all ten objects to within $\pm5$--$7\%$, and the best-window error correlates with $K_{\mathrm{eff}}$ at $-0.86$ to $-0.90$ (descriptive correlations over the ten-object panel; no resampled intervals) --- consistent, within this tested panel, with the object entering through $K_{\mathrm{eff}}$ alone, as Proposition~1 predicts to leading Gaussian/log-variance order. The shuffled-paired arm performing like the random arms bears out the permutation analysis of Sec.~7 end to end on this panel: pairing cancels the slow drift within pairs, and the permutation restores the stationarity that the ordering destroyed.

The 20-seed uncertainty replication (31{,}600 rows total) confirms these slopes under the fixed $W\le16$ variance-segment convention. Because the 20 drift seeds and 10 objects are each reused across the full grid, an object$\times$seed bootstrap that treats the 200 cells as independent understates uncertainty; the honest convention adopted here is the two-way (seeds-and-objects) crossed cluster bootstrap, under which the naive intervals reproduce the previously published ones (e.g.\ SRHT slope $-0.58$ $[-0.60,-0.54]$) but widen by a factor of $3$--$5$ without crossing any qualitative boundary. Median log--log slope with two-way 95\% CI is $-0.41$ $[-0.44,-0.37]$ for random binary, $-0.39$ $[-0.42,-0.31]$ for random uniform, $-0.58$ $[-0.66,-0.41]$ for SRHT-paired, and $-0.62$ $[-0.67,-0.44]$ for randomly permuted paired Hadamard, while the naturally ordered paired arm sits at $+0.03$ $[-0.06,+0.17]$ under the ratio AGC --- a CI straddling zero, i.e.\ no detectable decay under that estimator --- and the raw ordered arm's best-window error is $0.899$ $[0.892,0.903]$, essentially flat. One estimator-specific nuance is recorded: under the log-domain estimator the naturally ordered paired arm shows a shallow decay ($-0.222$ $[-0.306,-0.054]$) where the ratio AGC shows none; both remain far outside the $W^{-1/2}$ band, so the chronology conclusion stands, but ``no detectable decay'' attaches to the ratio-AGC estimator specifically. The corresponding best-window blind gain errors at this replication's operating point ($\rho_{\mathrm{pair}}=10^{-3}$, $s=0.1$) are reported in Table~1. It is worth noting that this replication's slope fits use the fixed $W\le16$ variance-segment convention, whereas the exploratory slopes and $R^2$ figures above used an adaptive segment; the two conventions agree on every qualitative conclusion.

\noindent\textbf{The reconstruction bridge (Fig.~4).} This experiment tests Theorem~1 as an identity rather than a bound, on a fully balanced 8100-row design: three reconstructors, three transform sizes $N\in\{1024,2048,4096\}$, six injected residual-gain variances $v$, ten objects, fifteen seeds. It can be observed that the deterministic inverses are flat in $N$, as $B_L=1$ demands: the per-$v$ log--log slopes have maximal magnitude $0.074$ (orthogonal) and $0.014$ (SRHT) --- no $1/N$ reconstruction floor. The random/DGI arm decays as $1/N$ at every $v$ (per-$v$ slopes $-1.031$ to $-0.974$), and its leverage law is quantitative: the measured relMSE agrees with $\mathrm{leverage}\times v$ within $[0.91, 1.06]$, with $\mathrm{leverage}\times N$ between $7.38\times10^{5}$ and $7.68\times10^{5}$ across the $N$ sweep (a $4\%$ drift). For the orthogonal inverse, $\mathrm{relMSE}/v \approx 1.0$ for 8 of 10 objects; the two DC-dominated objects --- natural\_patch at $0.35$ and stripe at $0.54$ --- fall below the $v$ line because the scale alignment of the metric absorbs the gain component co-linear with the object, an alignment effect rather than a failure of the identity. Fig.~4's numbers are accordingly read in the \emph{scale-aligned image metric}, and the deterministic (orthogonal/SRHT) arms and the random/DGI arm are read against different error conventions --- the direct inverse's $\mathrm{relMSE}/v$ against unit leverage versus the correlator's $\mathrm{leverage}\times N$ law --- so cross-arm error levels in Fig.~4 are not directly comparable; the raw-MSE identity-verification role common to all three reconstructors is carried by the injected-residual E4 comparison below.

\noindent\textbf{Phase diagram and ablation (Figs.~5 and 6).} The phase diagram sweeps the drift rate $\rho\in[10^{-3},10]$ and noise level $\sigma\in[0.05,0.5]$ at equal frames, with an above-floor gate $\mathrm{relMSE}<0.5$ separating cells where reconstruction is meaningful from cells at the noise floor --- the gate excludes reconstructions whose error energy is at least half the object energy, a conservative meaningfulness cut; cells failing it are reported as noise floor rather than assigned a winner. The equal-frame winner map has 45 cells (9 drift rates $\times$ 5 noise levels), of which 29 are above floor: SRHT-paired acquisition with pairwise correction wins 28 of the 29; one cell ($\rho_{\mathrm{pair}}=0.3$, $\sigma_a=0.15$) goes to randomly ordered pairwise Hadamard with the SCGI-proxy correction; the remaining 16 cells are labeled noise floor rather than assigned a winner. The boundary curves shown are two-parameter power-law fits ($R^2=0.989$--$0.9995$, fitted exponents $-0.94$ to $-1.93$) to the empirical SRHT-paired-versus-ordered-Hadamard crossovers --- a descriptive summary of a comparison that Prop~3 explicitly does not rank, not a test of Prop~3's closed form. The no-free-parameter test of Prop~3 is reported separately: on the same grid, with every constant measured ($C_0$, $K_{\mathrm{eff}}$, $D_H$, $s$, $N$) and a gain-known random arm ($v=0$), the predicted boundary $\rho^*=-\log\big(1-2(C_0/N)/(K_{\mathrm{eff}}D_Hs^2)\big)$ lands within a median factor $1.54$ of the 42 observed crossings (all within factor 2 for $\sigma_a\ge0.1$; empirical $\sigma$-exponent $-2.11$ vs.\ $-2.07$ predicted), systematically early. For the blind random arm, the residual-gain leverage in (F.12) must carry the raw-correlator constant $1+C_0^{\mathrm{raw}}$ of (F.10) ($\approx10^6$), not the mean-subtracted pipeline $C_0$ ($\approx7\times10^2$): mean subtraction removes the $K_{\mathrm{eff}}K(\mu/\sigma)^2$ background penalty from the sampling floor but \emph{not} from the drift leverage, a $\sim1.4\times10^3$ correction verified to 5\% at white drift. With the corrected constant the theory predicts --- and the data confirm, 100/100 cells --- that blind random+DGI never overtakes ordered pairwise Hadamard anywhere on this grid; the intermediate-drift winner is SRHT-paired acquisition, which avoids the $C_0/N$ floor entirely. The ablation isolates the mechanism at the single above-floor drift point $\rho=10^{-3}$: SRHT gains $+5.45$ dB over ordered Hadamard, while pixel-sign randomization alone gains $+5.47$ dB and acquisition-order permutation alone $+5.39$ dB --- each ingredient by itself recovers essentially the full advantage, and combining them is not additive; these dB gains are descriptive values at this single grid point (no resampled intervals). It is emphasized that Fig.~6's arms randomize the acquisition-time structure only: its permutation arms reorder the Hadamard rows ($P_{\mathrm{row}}$) and its combined arm is $P_{\mathrm{row}}{+}D$ --- no Fig.~6 arm applies the pixel permutation $P_{\mathrm{col}}$ of the Appendix~E analysis, whose factorized attribution is reported in Sec.~9.6. What matters empirically is that the chronology is randomized, not the particular form of the randomization. At the largest simulated adjacent-pair decorrelation rates ($\rho\ge1$), all blind variants empirically approach the noise floor within $\pm0.09$ dB; this is consistent with the information-loss intuition behind the $p\to N$ algebraic limit of Theorem~A$'$, but it is not a direct test of the low-pass dimension threshold unless gain bandwidth ($\rho_{\mathrm{bw}}$) and adjacent-pair decorrelation ($\rho_{\mathrm{pair}}$) are controlled separately.

\noindent\textbf{Low-photon robustness (Fig.~7).} This experiment tests the soft-log analysis of Sec.~6 under Poisson counting, on identical random-basis carriers and shared gain traces, with four estimator arms on the same paired Poisson draw at each budget: the naive clipped log, the Anscombe transform, the uncalibrated soft-log proxy (windowed mean of $\psi_\alpha$ with no calibration inversion), and the calibrated soft-log of Theorem~C, $\hat\theta=m_\alpha^{-1}(\mathrm{mean}_W\,\psi_\alpha)$. Every ratio below names its numerator and denominator. As shown in Fig.~7, at mean counts $\bar\lambda\le1$ the naive clipped log is $22.9$--$46.4\times$ worse than the uncalibrated proxy, $19.5$--$30.0\times$ worse than Anscombe, and $3.8$--$10.7\times$ worse than the calibrated estimator (all naive-over-method mean-MSE ratios). The calibrated estimator is the only arm that tracks the Fisher-matched $1/(W\bar\lambda)$ law across the variance regime: fitted log--log slope $-0.974$ over $\bar\lambda\in[2,32]$ and $-0.981$ over $[1,16]$, against $-0.910$ and $-0.730$ for the proxy, whose $[1,16]$ fit is contaminated by its own bias transition. Below $\bar\lambda\sim1$ the proxy and Anscombe fall \emph{below} the unbiased (local) Fisher reference --- a shrinkage-bias signature, not super-efficiency --- whereas the calibrated estimator is never sub-Fisher, staying within $3$--$31\%$ above the reference at $\bar\lambda\le1$. The proxy's apparent $1.18$--$1.55\times$ mean-MSE advantage over Anscombe at these budgets is that shrinkage bias, not an efficiency gain; and the calibrated estimator does \emph{not} beat Anscombe in mean MSE at any tested budget --- the Anscombe-over-calibrated ratio is $0.13$--$0.55$ at $\bar\lambda\le1$, i.e.\ Anscombe is $1.8$--$7.8\times$ better there, a bias-bought advantage that is exactly the trade-off of the Sec.~6 caveat. Theorem~C's variance claim is thereby confirmed by its own estimator, with no bias crossover to excuse; all pairwise ratios converge to $1.00$ (within $\pm4\%$) for $\bar\lambda\ge32$. At the high-photon end the floor is drift-limited rather than photon-limited: the gain MSE of $2.78\times10^{-4}$ at $\rho=10^{-3}$ falls to $1.73\times10^{-4}$ when the drift slows to $\rho=10^{-4}$, identically for the proxy and the calibrated arm.

\noindent\textbf{Threshold, whitening-power, and accountability verifications (Fig.~8 and Supplementary Material S1--S4).} A series of five further experiments closes the remaining theory--evidence gaps. Unless a bootstrap interval is stated, every number in this subsection is a descriptive result of its specified grid, with no resampled intervals.

\emph{Tall-design threshold scan (Fig.~8).} A direct test of the local component of Theorem~A$'$ at two object sizes: $K=64$ (20 seeds per cell, 1300 cells) and $K=128$ (30 seeds per cell, 1950 cells), each over five low-pass drift dimensions $p\in\{3,5,9,17,33\}$ with frame counts swept through the wall at margins $N-K-p\in[-8,+16]$. As shown in Fig.~8, at both sizes the local rank test on the collision Jacobian transitions from $0\%$ to $100\%$ pass \emph{exactly} at margin $N-K-p=-1$ for every $p$ (3250 cells, no exceptions) --- matching the proved a.e.\ rank formula $\operatorname{rank}D\Phi_M=\min\{N,K+p-1\}$ of Theorem~A$'$(i), whose local rank wall $N=K+p-1$ is now an if-and-only-if theorem for $K\ge2$; below the wall the observed rank deficits correspond, by the same theorem, to genuine positive-dimensional families of exact aliases, not merely to numerical degeneracy. Being a rank statement, the scan verifies the local component and probes algorithmic recovery; it neither tests the global sufficiency thresholds of A$'$(ii)--(iii) nor their conjectured sharpness. The companion solver panel (blind alternating recovery, 8 restarts $\times$ 150 iterations) starts at the same wall but climbs slowly --- about $2\%$ success at margin $0$ and only $\sim50\%$ at margin $+16$ for $K=64$, slower still at $K=128$ ($\sim41\%$ at margin $+16$) --- an \emph{algorithmic-recovery} observation, not a uniqueness test: uniqueness and recovery separate exactly as the third notion of Sec.~2 anticipates, with the gap widening as the dimension grows (see also Sec.~10).

\emph{Multi-permutation whitening power and factorized randomization ablation (Supplementary Material).} Two randomization semantics must be kept separate (Appendix~E.6): the pixel-level randomization of the SRHT theory --- pixel permutation $P_{\mathrm{col}}$ and pixel signs $D$, whose Lemma~E.1 covariance is a function of the permuted squared object alone and hence invariant to acquisition order --- and the acquisition-order reordering $P_{\mathrm{row}}$, which controls temporal stationarity of the acquired time series; the Brown--Forsythe screen probes the latter. The historical whitening-power arm (32 draws $\times$ 10 objects under the Fig.~2 protocol) applied a pixel permutation \emph{and} an independent acquisition-order permutation simultaneously: its rejection rate at $p<10^{-3}$ is $19/320=5.9\%$, against $70\%$ (7 of 10 objects) for the ordered reference arm. The factorized ablation attributes this outcome: $P_{\mathrm{col}}$ alone rejects at $5.6\%$ and $P_{\mathrm{row}}$ alone at $12.2\%$ --- either permutation alone suffices for the stationarity screen --- whereas pixel signs $D$ alone do not ($43.4\%$): sign flips whiten carrier \emph{amplitudes} but leave the coarse-to-fine sequency-energy ordering of natural-Hadamard rows intact. The theory's own randomization $P_{\mathrm{col}}{+}D$, in natural acquisition order, passes at $11.9\%$, so the whitening claims of Appendix~E attach to the theory's randomization and do not depend on the row-order confound; the code's SRHT arm ($P_{\mathrm{row}}{+}D$) passes at $5.9\%$. The natural per-draw certificate candidate fails: the maximum non-DC Walsh coefficient of the permuted $T^2$ does \emph{not} predict which draws reject (Spearman $0.092$, $p=0.10$) --- an honest null, consistent with the probabilistic (not per-draw-certifiable) nature of permutation whitening (Sec.~7).

\emph{Coherent-residual validation of Theorem~1 (E4, Supplementary Material).} Five injected residual-covariance structures --- i.i.d., AR(1), sinusoidal, low-pass OU, blockwise --- at fixed $v=10^{-3}$, across three reconstructors and ten objects (150 cells), measured in the raw-residual (identity-verification) metric rather than the scale-aligned image metric of Fig.~4. The full matrix law of Theorem~1 tracks the measured relMSE within $4.2\%$ across all 150 cells. The scalar $v\,B_L$ law is exact for the complete orthogonal and SRHT inversions --- for these, $LL^{\top}\propto I/N$ analytically, so the gain term feels only $\mathrm{diag}(V_\delta)$ --- but fails by up to $3.65\times$ for random/DGI under low-pass-coherent residuals: a direct verification of Theorem~1's coherent clause. It is worth noting that E4 validates the matrix law under \emph{injected} residuals; it does not probe the same-record $\delta$--$\xi$ dependence flagged after Theorem~1.

\emph{$C_0$ audit (Supplementary Material).} The one-sample constant measured from $2\times10^{4}$ pattern draws per ensemble agrees with the moment formula of Appendix~F to $0.35\%$ mean ($2.01\%$ worst) relative error across four pattern ensembles $\times$ five objects. The strong ensemble dependence --- $O(K)$ for signed Rademacher patterns versus $O(K_{\mathrm{eff}}K)$ for the nonnegative offset ensembles --- confirms that $C_0$ is pipeline-specific, as Theorem~1's DGI specialization insists.

\emph{Oracle, metered, and blind baselines (Supplementary Material).} On four held-out objects with OU drift ($\rho=10^{-3}$, $s=0.1$), the accountability ordering static $\ge$ oracle $\ge$ blind-AGC $\ge$ no-correction holds in CNR for 4 of 4 objects, with an oracle$-$blind CNR gap of $0.035$--$0.147$: at this drift point the blind windowed estimator concedes little to a gain oracle, while forgoing correction altogether costs the most.

\section{Discussion: significance, scope, and outlook}

The results of Sections~2--9 are, at bottom, a set of pre-acquisition design tools: quantities one can evaluate on the illumination basis and the expected drift \emph{before} photons are collected, and which predict whether blind gain--object separation will succeed. They are collected here as four deliverables, followed by their limits and the experimental program they invite.

\noindent\textbf{A go/no-go identifiability predictor.} The tall-design thresholds convert ``will this acquisition self-calibrate?'' into arithmetic. For any fixed drift class $S\ni\mathbf 1$ of dimension $p=\dim S$ --- including the physical low-pass family --- generic local identifiability holds iff $N\ge K+p-1$ (for $K\ge2$; the $K=1$ stratum is globally identifiable on the nonvanishing-carrier set at every admissible $N$), generic exact finite-sample identifiability holds for almost every design--object pair at $N\ge K+p$, and uniform identifiability over every object with nonvanishing carrier at $N\ge 2K+p-1$ (Theorem~A$'$ --- proved sufficiency directions; sharpness of the two global thresholds remains a labeled prediction). Written through the bandwidth relation $p\approx\rho_{\mathrm{bw}}N$, the generic threshold becomes $N\gtrsim K/(1-\rho_{\mathrm{bw}})$: slow drift needs only modest oversampling, while $\rho_{\mathrm{bw}}\to1$ ($p\to N$) makes blind algebraic separation impossible for any $N$, forcing reliance on the statistical anchor of Theorem~B --- which itself degrades once the gain decorrelates within the averaging window (the $\rho\ge1$ convergence of Fig.~6). This is a regime map the experimenter reads off before building.

\noindent\textbf{An SRHT design rule.} When blind slow gain is the operative nuisance, do not run ordered Hadamard: its chronological coefficient ordering hands the object a temporal signature indistinguishable from drift. Randomize the carrier instead --- via sign and permutation randomization of a Walsh--Hadamard design, keeping the two permutation semantics distinct (Appendix~E.6). The ingredients play distinct roles and should not be compressed into one guarantee: a \emph{pixel} permutation $P_{\mathrm{col}}$ enters the covariance-whitening guarantee --- sign flips whiten if and only if the realized (permuted) $T^2$ is Walsh-flat (Sec.~7), an event the pixel permutation makes likely once $\min(\varepsilon^2 K_4,\varepsilon K_\infty)\ge C\log(K/\delta)$ --- while an \emph{acquisition-order} reordering $P_{\mathrm{row}}$ makes the temporal carrier exchangeable, the anchor mechanism of Sec.~5; empirically, either permutation alone passes the temporal-stationarity screen, whereas signs alone do not (Sec.~9.6). Retain a positivity/offset margin, and choose the gain window $W$ from the bias--variance tradeoff of the proposed windowed estimator. Under those checks, the proposed acquisition rule buys statistical self-calibration without surrendering orthogonal conditioning.

\noindent\textbf{A design map that was exposed to falsification.} The master finite-noise identity (Theorem~1) and the finite-$N$ flip boundary $\rho^*$ (Prop~3) render relMSE across DGI, SRHT, and pairwise-Hadamard as a chart over basis, drift, and noise (Fig.~5). It is worth noting that $\rho_{\mathrm{pair}}$ (adjacent-pair decorrelation, entering $\rho^*$) and $\rho_{\mathrm{bw}}$ (bandwidth, entering the tall-design law) are distinct axes and must be labeled as such before any overlay (Remark, Sec.~8). The boundary prediction was exposed to falsification, with a two-regime outcome (Sec.~9.4): the gain-known skeleton of Prop~3 is validated with zero fitted parameters (median agreement factor $1.54$; $\sigma$-exponent $-2.11$ measured against $-2.07$ predicted), while the blind-arm flip prediction as originally written was falsified --- the drift-leverage constant must be the raw-correlator $1+C_0^{\mathrm{raw}}$, a $\sim1.4\times10^3$ correction, after which the theory predicts, and the data confirm in 100/100 cells, no flip for the mean-subtracted blind pipeline on the simulated grid. The failure was localized to one measurable constant, not to the functional form: mean subtraction removes the background from the sampling floor but not from the drift leverage. That diagnosis is itself a product of the accountability pipeline, and it sharpens the paper's central bookkeeping rule --- every constant is pipeline-specific, and the sampling-floor and drift-leverage constants of the \emph{same} pipeline can differ by three orders of magnitude.

\noindent\textbf{A cross-domain principle.} The exportable statement is basis-agnostic: \emph{to self-calibrate a multiplicative nuisance, design the carrier so its distribution is stationary in the nuisance coordinate.} The following are offered as analogues and motivation, not as proven domains --- each would require its own operator analysis of the diagonal-row map \cite{LiLeeBresler1501.06120,KechKrahmer1603.07316}: blind gain and phase calibration, flat-field correction, coded-aperture and computational microscopy, and single-pixel spectroscopy all share the bilinear structure in which this reasoning applies.

\noindent\textbf{Scope and limits.} The claims are theory and simulation only, with no hardware validation. Uniqueness near $N=K+p$ is an identifiability statement, not a conditioning guarantee; stability there is a separate random-matrix question, and the sharpness of the two global identifiability thresholds remains a labeled prediction (Sec.~4). Random square unconstrained designs are \emph{not} algebraically identifiable --- any invertible design absorbs nonconstant gain into a redefined object (Theorem~A). There is no universal DGI constant $C_0$ or Hadamard factor $D_H$; both must be measured per pipeline --- and within one pipeline the sampling-floor and drift-leverage constants differ once a background is subtracted (Sec.~8). The low-photon soft-log $\psi_\alpha$ avoids the log-zero singularity but cannot manufacture information --- zero photons carry zero Fisher information for log-intensity, an information limit, not an estimator defect.

\noindent\textbf{Experimental feasibility.} The theory issues sharp, falsifiable predictions for a bench with programmable drift: the gain-known flip point $\rho^*$ of Prop~3, the no-flip prediction for mean-subtracted blind DGI pipelines, the empirical drift-rate crossover at which SRHT-paired carriers overtake ordered pairwise Hadamard, and an SRHT-versus-ordered-Hadamard separation in blind gain-estimation error under stirred-scattering gain (Fig.~3). Testing these against a physical single-pixel system --- with real read noise, Poisson statistics, and unmodeled gain jumps --- is left to future hardware work, likely in collaboration with an optical-imaging group.

In conclusion, we have established an identifiability theory of blind gain--object separation for single-pixel acquisition: the square-design obstruction and the tall-design thresholds (Theorems A and A$'$), the stationarity anchor with its minimax-rate-optimal windowed estimator (Theorems B--D), the SRHT synthesis (Sec.~7), and the finite-noise reconstruction bridge with its flip-boundary phase diagram (Theorem~1, Prop~3). Taken together, these tools make gain observable not by measuring it directly, but by making the object stop pretending to be time. The proposed framework opens an avenue for self-calibrating acquisition design in real-world scenarios, e.g., single-pixel imaging through dynamic and complex scattering media.
