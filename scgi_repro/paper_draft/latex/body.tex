\section{Introduction}

Single-pixel (ghost) imaging \cite{Pittman1995PRA,Bennink2002PRL,Shapiro2008PRA,Bromberg2009PRA} has become the method of choice for imaging through media that defeat conventional cameras \cite{Bina2013PRL,Cheng2009OE}: turbid water, fog, smoke, and scattering channels around corners \cite{PengChen2024APL,PengXiaoChen2025OE}. The instrument is disarmingly simple---a series of structured illumination patterns and a bucket detector \cite{Duarte2008SPM,Edgar2019NatPhoton}---and the textbook design instinct is equally simple: use an ordered orthogonal basis. A Hadamard scan acquires exactly one transform coefficient per frame \cite{Zhang2017OE,Yu2019Sensors}, the measurement matrix is perfectly conditioned, and an exact inverse is waiting at the end of the acquisition. Against additive noise, this is provably the safe choice; nothing about the pattern set is left to chance.

Dynamic media break this instinct in a specific and, as will be argued, instructive way. A time-varying channel multiplies the bucket record by a slowly drifting gain: the $n$-th measurement is $R_n = a_n B_n$, where $B_n$ is the object-dependent bucket coefficient and $a_n > 0$ is an unknown per-frame gain with log-gain $\ell_n = \log a_n$ confined to a slow-drift class $S$ of dimension $p$. Recent experimental work on ghost imaging through dynamic and complex scattering media \cite{PengChen2024APL} and on optical wireless transmission around corners \cite{PengXiaoChen2025OE} shows that self-calibrating schemes built on \emph{random} illumination with an empirical correction of dynamic scaling factors succeed where ordered scans fail. Read naively, this is a puzzle: the ordered Hadamard scan is the better-conditioned instrument, yet randomness---usually a concession, not a feature---wins. Therefore, it is desirable to determine when, and by what mechanism, a drifting gain becomes blindly separable at all.

The resolution is that under gain drift the ordering itself becomes the liability. An ordered scan sorts the object's transform coefficients into a smooth sequence in acquisition time, so the object acquires a \emph{temporal signature}: a slow gain trace and a slow coefficient trace cast the same shadow on the one-dimensional bucket record, and no estimator can tell them apart from that record alone. Hadamard is not defeated by randomness; it is defeated by chronology. It is shown that this confounding is not an artifact of any particular correction algorithm but a genuine algebraic ambiguity---for a square unconstrained design, \emph{any} invertible pattern matrix can absorb a nonconstant gain into a perfectly valid alternative object (Theorem~A). Conversely, random carriers escape it by a statistical mechanism: when the pattern sequence makes the bucket carrier's distribution stationary in time, slow gain becomes the only non-stationary component of the record, and the relative gain becomes identifiable up to the unavoidable global scale (Theorem~B). Self-calibration is thus a property one can \emph{design into acquisition time}, and its price, rates, and failure boundaries can be computed in advance.

In this paper, we develop that observation into a quantitative identifiability theory. The contributions are:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{A three-notion identifiability framework} for the bilinear gain--object model $R_n = a_n B_n$, separating exact-algebraic identifiability, statistical (asymptotic) identifiability, and estimation conditioning. Within it, the square-design obstruction (Theorem~A) is proved, and the tall-design thresholds (Theorem~A$'$)---generic exact identifiability at $N \ge K + p$ and uniform identifiability at $N \ge 2K + p - 1$---are established at generic-pair level for the physical Fourier low-pass drift family, with the uniform-over-drift reading resting on a labeled dimension-count argument plus an exact empirical rank transition (Secs.~4, 9). This is distinct from generic bilinear-injectivity results \cite{KechKrahmer1603.07316,LiLeeBresler1501.06120,AhmedRechtRomberg1211.5608,ChoudharyMitra1402.2637,KuengRauhutTerstiege1410.6913}: the operator here is a diagonal-row map, and the anchor is temporal, not structural.

\item \textbf{The stationarity anchor (Theorem~B)} and its consistency condition ($\star$): random carriers pin the relative gain statistically, with a windowed log-domain estimator achieving the minimax rate over the drift class, including a photon-limited variant whose error scales as $1/(W\bar\lambda)$ for windows $W$.

\item \textbf{A synthesis, not a trade-off:} randomized Hadamard (SRHT) designs retain orthogonal conditioning while randomizing the temporal carrier --- the stationary/exchangeable carrier being supplied under Walsh-flatness of $T^2$ or a successful random permutation of a sufficiently spread object --- with an exact Walsh-domain necessary-and-sufficient whitening condition separating the roles of sign flips and permutations.

\item \textbf{An exact finite-noise reconstruction bridge} (Theorem~1) expressing relative MSE for any linear reconstructor through a leverage functional $B_L$, covering residual gain, read noise, and Poisson noise without Gaussian approximation, and specializing to orthogonal, pairwise-Hadamard, and random/DGI pipelines \cite{Ferri2010PRL,Sun2012OE} (with the DGI constant $C_0$ measured per implementation---no universal value exists).

\item \textbf{A phase diagram with a finite-$N$ flip boundary} $\rho^*$ in the drift-decorrelation parameter $\rho_{\mathrm{pair}}$ (Prop~3), giving a pre-acquisition go/no-go design chart (Fig.~5) for choosing between ordered and randomized acquisition.
\end{enumerate}

The scope is emphasized: all results are theoretical, supported by numerical evidence; no hardware validation is reported. Identifiability near $N = K + p$ is a uniqueness statement, not a conditioning guarantee, and random square designs remain algebraically non-identifiable---their advantage is statistical. The organizing idea throughout is the distilled form of the paradox: one makes the gain observable not by measuring it directly, but by making the object stop pretending to be time.

The paper is organized as follows. Section~2 fixes the model and the three notions of identifiability; Fig.~1 places ordered-Hadamard, iid-random, tall-random, and SRHT designs on the resulting two-axis map of conditioning versus identifiability. Section~3 positions the work against bilinear inverse-problem theory. Sections~4 and 5 establish the algebraic obstruction and the stationarity anchor; Sec.~6 derives estimation rates including the low-photon regime. Section~7 develops the SRHT synthesis, Sec.~8 the reconstruction bridge and phase diagram. Section~9 presents the numerical experiments, and Sec.~10 collects design rules, scope, and outlook.

\section{Problem setup and three notions of identifiability}

We consider a single-pixel (bucket-detection) acquisition of $N$ frames. On frame $n$ a programmed pattern $m_n \in \mathbb{R}^K$ illuminates a static object $T \in \mathbb{R}^K$, producing the ideal bucket value $B_n = m_n^{\mathsf T} T$; we call the sequence $\{B_n\}$ the \emph{object carrier}. The recorded signal is corrupted by a slowly drifting multiplicative gain (source power, detector responsivity, coupling efficiency) and can be described by

\[
R_n \;=\; a_n B_n, \qquad a_n = e^{\ell_n} > 0 ,
\]

where $a_n$ denotes the unknown per-frame gain and the log-gain trajectory $\ell = (\ell_1,\dots,\ell_N)$ is confined to a \emph{drift class} $S \subset \mathbb{R}^N$, $p = \dim S$, which always contains the constants. The constant direction is a pure gauge: $(a, T) \mapsto (c\,a,\; T/c)$ leaves every $R_n$ invariant, so all statements below are up to a global scale. For physically slow drift we take $S$ to be a low-pass (Fourier) space of normalized bandwidth $\rho_{\mathrm{bw}}$, so that $p \approx \rho_{\mathrm{bw}} N$; this bandwidth parameter must not be confused with the adjacent-pair decorrelation $\rho_{\mathrm{pair}}$ entering the finite-$N$ flip boundary of Sec.~8. The blind problem --- recover $(a, T)$ from $\{R_n\}$ alone --- is bilinear, and asking whether it is ``solvable'' turns out to be three distinct questions. Separating them is itself a contribution of this paper.

\begin{nameddef}{Conventions (fixed throughout)}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
\item $K$, $T$ --- number of pixels; object $T\in\mathbb{R}^K$, entrywise nonnegative unless stated otherwise.
\item $N$ --- number of acquired frames.
\item $S$ --- linear log-gain drift class; \textbf{always contains the constants}.
\item $p=\dim S$ --- drift dimension; the \emph{nonconstant} drift directions number $p-1$.
\item global scale --- \textbf{all} identifiability statements are modulo the gauge $(a,T)\mapsto(c\,a,\,T/c)$; we fix the gauge by centering the log-gain ($\overline{\ell}=0$); reconstruction error metrics use least-squares scale alignment (Sec.~8).
\item identifiability notions --- \emph{local-differential}, \emph{generic-exact}, and \emph{uniform-exact} identifiability are distinct properties with distinct thresholds (Theorem~A$'$); none of them implies conditioning.
\item $\rho_{\mathrm{bw}}$ --- normalized gain \textbf{bandwidth}: $p\approx\rho_{\mathrm{bw}}N$ (tall-design law, Sec.~4).
\item $\rho_{\mathrm{pair}}$ --- \textbf{adjacent-pair} gain decorrelation, entering $\mathrm{Var}(\Delta)\approx 2s^2 r(\rho_{\mathrm{pair}})$ (flip boundary, Sec.~8); related to, but not equal to, $\rho_{\mathrm{bw}}$.
\item $K_{\mathrm{eff}}=S_1^2/S_2$ --- excitation functional, with $S_1=\sum_j T_j$, $S_2=\sum_j T_j^2$.
\item $K_\infty=S_2/\|T\|_\infty^2$ --- Lindeberg spikiness functional (Gaussian/log-domain approximations).
\item $K_4=S_2^2/\sum_j T_j^4$ --- spectral-spread functional (permutation whitening, Sec.~7).
\item $C_0$ --- one-sample DGI correlator constant, $C_0=\mathbb{E}\|Z\|^2/S_2-1$; pipeline-specific, no universal value.
\item $D_H$ --- pairwise-Hadamard factor, $\approx 1$ for non-DC coefficients; measured per pipeline.
\item $B_L$ --- leverage of a linear reconstructor $L$, $B_L=(1/S_2)\sum_n b_n^2\|Le_n\|^2$ (Sec.~8).
\end{itemize}
\end{nameddef}

Pattern entries $m_n$ may be signed (Hadamard) or nonnegative (speckle) even though $T$ is nonnegative; where signed coefficients appear as carriers, the bucket conventions of Sec.~5 specify how they are physically realized.

\noindent\textbf{Algebraic identifiability.} Does the noiseless record determine $(a,T)$ up to gauge, or does some other pair $(a', T')$ with $\ell' - \ell \in S$ nonconstant reproduce the data exactly? This is a finite-sample, deterministic question about collisions $MT = D_s M T'$, and its answer depends on the design $M$ and on the dimension count $N$ versus $K + p$. Section~4 (Theorem~A and the tall-design thresholds, Theorem~A$'$) shows that every square invertible design --- however well-conditioned --- fails it, and that overdetermination restores it generically at $N \ge K + p$.

\noindent\textbf{Statistical identifiability.} When the patterns are drawn from a random ensemble, a weaker but operationally decisive property becomes available: if the carrier $B_n$ is \emph{stationary} --- its distribution does not depend on $n$ --- then any slow modulation of local averages over windows $W$ is attributable to the gain alone, and the relative gain is identifiable in distribution even where exact algebraic separation is impossible. This stationarity anchor is developed in Sec.~5 (Theorem~B) and is the mechanism the field's empirical successes have been implicitly exploiting.

\noindent\textbf{Estimation conditioning.} Uniqueness is not accuracy. Even when one of the first two notions holds, the finite-noise error of a concrete estimator is governed by conditioning quantities --- the window size $W$, the residual-gain variance $v$, the leverage $B_L$ of the reconstruction operator --- quantified by the rate analysis of Sec.~6 and the reconstruction bridge of Sec.~8. In particular, identifiability at the threshold $N = K + p$ says nothing about stability there.

We contend that much of the confusion surrounding blind gain correction stems from conflating these three notions: judging an acquisition scheme by the visual quality of a blindly corrected reconstruction entangles algebraic ambiguity, the presence or absence of a statistical anchor, and estimator conditioning into a single scalar, although the three failures call for entirely different remedies. Figure~1 disentangles them as a two-axis map --- inversion conditioning against blind identifiability --- on which ordered Hadamard, i.i.d.-random (DGI), tall random, and SRHT acquisitions occupy visibly different corners, a separation that Secs.~4 and 5 explain.

\section{Relation to prior work}

Gain--object separation is a bilinear inverse problem: writing the log-gain as $\ell_n=\log a_n$ and lifting the unknowns to the rank-one array $X=aT^{\top}$, each bucket reads $R_n=a_n B_n$ with $B_n=m_n^{\top}T$, a bilinear form in the object $T$ and the gain trace $a$. This places our question inside a mature literature on bilinear identifiability, and it is worth stating precisely what that literature settles and what it leaves open for the diagonal-gain geometry considered here.

The sharpest injectivity counts for lifted bilinear maps are Kech--Krahmer's optimal conditions for generic identifiability \cite{KechKrahmer1603.07316}, which for a rank-one measurement model require on the order of $2(n_1+n_2)-4$ generic measurements. These counts do not transfer to our setting: the operator $L_M(X)_n=a_n\,m_n^{\top}T$ is a \emph{diagonal-row} map, not a generic rank-one measurement, so the object carrier and the gain enter through a structured --- not generic --- set of linear forms. The relevant identifiability question is instead the intersection geometry of the object subspace with its gain-modulated copy, $\,U\cap D_sU$. Choudhary--Mitra's scaling laws for bilinear inverse problems \cite{ChoudharyMitra1402.2637} are the closest in spirit to our Theorem~A: their rank-two nullspace obstruction is exactly the mechanism by which a square unconstrained design absorbs a nonconstant gain into a redefined object. Li--Lee--Bresler's unified framework \cite{LiLeeBresler1501.06120} supplies the subspace- and sparsity-constrained identifiability thresholds that our tall-design counts ($N\ge K+p$ generic, $N\ge 2K+p-1$ uniform) specialize to for the diagonal-gain model. Ahmed--Recht--Romberg \cite{AhmedRechtRomberg1211.5608} establish convex recovery for blind deconvolution under subspace priors, and Kueng--Rauhut--Terstiege \cite{KuengRauhutTerstiege1410.6913} give the low-rank-from-rank-one-measurement guarantees; both are the algorithmic backdrop against which our purely algebraic and statistical criteria should be read.

On the imaging side, the practice of single-pixel and computational ghost imaging has long weighed ordered Hadamard bases against random speckle patterns for their conditioning and noise properties, but without an identifiability account of slow multiplicative drift. Learned correction of dynamic scaling factors in ghost imaging \cite{PengChen2024APL} and learned encoding for optical wireless links through complex channels \cite{PengXiaoChen2025OE} address the same physical nuisance empirically; our analysis explains why their \emph{static-correction ceiling} --- the error floor left by any time-independent correction (one learned or fitted on static data) when applied to a drifting record --- is the honest performance bound, and when a drifting gain becomes blindly separable at all. Blind self-calibration of unknown sensor gains and phases has a long history outside imaging --- from array-processing eigenstructure methods \cite{WeissFriedlander1990} to blind calibration of sensor networks \cite{Balzano2007IPSN} and biconvex compressive self-calibration \cite{LingStrohmer2015} --- and Balzano--Nowak's blind sensor calibration similarly finds gains recoverable under mild oversampling, which our tall-design threshold $N\ge K+p$ makes exact for the diagonal bilinear model. On the acquisition-design side, pink-noise speckle illumination \cite{Nie2021PRA} and sparse Hadamard sequencing \cite{Han2025OE} optimize the pattern set directly, but target additive-noise robustness, whereas our mechanism concerns multiplicative-drift identifiability.

Against this background the contribution of the present work is specific. We do not add to generic bilinear injectivity, which is settled by the references above. What is new is the temporal-statistical mechanism: relative-gain identifiability secured by making the object carrier stationary (Theorem~B), the associated nonparametric slow-gain calibration rate, the ordered-orthogonal confounding failure mode that this mechanism repairs, the SRHT synthesis that recovers orthogonal conditioning without sacrificing self-calibration, and the finite-noise reconstruction bridge that renders the phase diagram quantitative. The novelty is the stationarity anchor, not bilinear injectivity per se.

\section{The algebraic obstruction and when oversampling cures it}

The paradox of Sec.~1 is not an estimator artifact. Write the noiseless bucket record as $R_n = a_n B_n$ with $B_n = m_n^{\mathsf T}T$ the carrier coefficient of the object $T\in\mathbb R^{K}$ under illumination row $m_n$, and $\ell_n=\log a_n$ the log-gain. Stack $N=K$ frames into an invertible square design $M$. Then the multiplicative drift is absorbed \emph{exactly} into a relabelled object.

\begin{namedthm}{Theorem A (square non-identifiability)}
For any invertible $M\in\mathbb R^{K\times K}$ and any gain $a=(a_n)$ nonconstant on the support of the carrier $MT$, there exists a distinct object $T'\neq cT$ producing the identical bucket record: $\operatorname{diag}(a)MT = M T'$ with $T'=M^{-1}\operatorname{diag}(a)MT$. Gain--object separation is unidentifiable up to the global-scale gauge $a\mapsto ca,\ T\mapsto T/c$. (Proof: Appendix~A.)
\end{namedthm}

The object is a barcode printed onto time and the drift is a smudge across it; when the barcode carries slow structure, the theorem says the smudge can be \emph{reread as a different barcode} rather than dismissed as blur. This is a genuine orbit of the bilinear map, and it holds for \emph{every} invertible design --- Hadamard, Fourier, or otherwise. Two corollaries sharpen the trap.

\begin{namedthm}{Corollary 1 (slowness does not help)}
Restricting $\ell\in S$ to a slow drift class does not restore identifiability at $N=K$: for any nonconstant $s\in S$, the alternative pair $\big(\ell-s,\; T'=M^{-1}\operatorname{diag}(e^{s})MT\big)$ explains the record exactly, and the alternative log-gain $\ell-s$ is again an admissible slow drift because $S$ is a linear space. A slowly varying gain thus masquerades as a slowly varying object.
\end{namedthm}

\begin{namedthm}{Corollary 2 (positivity is not enough; known support zeros can be)}
Nonnegativity of $T$ alone does not break the orbit. \emph{Known} zeros in the object support \emph{can}: each enforced zero removes a degree of freedom from $T'$, and $K_0\ge p-1$ generic zeros at known support locations restore \emph{local} identifiability via a rank condition on the constrained collision map --- one per nonconstant drift dimension, $p-1=\dim(S/\mathrm{span}\{1\})$. This is a local rank criterion, not a global guarantee. (Proof: Appendix~A.)
\end{namedthm}

The first escape is overdetermination. Take a generic tall design $M\in\mathbb R^{N\times K}$, $N>K$, with $\ell\in S$, $\dim S=p$, $S\ni$ constants (the gauge). A collision $MT=\operatorname{diag}(e^{s})MT'$ with $s=\ell'-\ell$ is governed by the intersection $\mathrm{range}(M)\cap\operatorname{diag}(e^{s})\,\mathrm{range}(M)$. Counting the projective incidence dimension of diagonal-gain collisions gives $\Delta = (p-1) + 2K-1-\operatorname{rank}[M,\,-\operatorname{diag}(b)M]$, with the rank equal to $\min(N,2K)$ on the dense stratum; generic-point identifiability requires $\Delta<K-1$ and uniform identifiability $\Delta<0$. This yields three thresholds.

\begin{namedthm}{Theorem A$'$ (tall-design thresholds)}
For generic tall $M$ and transverse nonconstant gain family of dimension $p$:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item local differential identifiability: $N \ge K+p-1$;
\item generic exact finite-sample identifiability: $N \ge K+p$;
\item uniform (every nonzero object): $N \ge 2K+p-1$.
\end{itemize}
(Proof: Appendix~B.)
\end{namedthm}

For the \emph{physical} low-pass drift space these bounds hold at three distinct levels of rigor, which we keep separate. For $S_{\mathrm{LP}}=\mathrm{span}\{1,\cos q\omega,\sin q\omega:q\le m\}$, $p=2m+1$, a level-multiplicity lemma (a nonconstant degree-$2m$ trigonometric polynomial attains each value at most $p-1$ times) bounds the collision rank, giving --- for each \emph{fixed} nonconstant $s\in S_{\mathrm{LP}}$ and generic $M$ --- $\operatorname{rank}[M,-\operatorname{diag}(b)M]=\min(N,2K)$ whenever $N\ge K+p$: the clean thresholds are thus \emph{proven at generic-pair level} --- for a generic pair $(M,s)$ --- for the Fourier low-pass family. The \emph{uniform-over-drift} version (one generic $M$ serving every nonconstant $s\in S_{\mathrm{LP}}$ simultaneously) rests on a dimension-count argument, labeled as such in Appendix~B: under a free determinantal codimension estimate it covers $N\le 2K-p+1$ and $N\ge 2K+p-1$, leaving the gap window $2K-p+1<N<2K+p-1$ at generic-pair level only. Empirically, the $K=64$ and $K=128$ threshold scans of Sec.~9 (Fig.~8) show an exact $0\%\to100\%$ rank transition at $N=K+p-1$ across all five drift dimensions tested at both object sizes (3250 cells in total, no exceptions), consistent with the uniform reading at the local level. (Proof: Appendix~B.) For other conventions one checks the level count $L_S\le K$ (if $N<2K$) or $L_S\le N-K$ (if $N\ge 2K$). Throughout, the middle threshold of Theorem~A$'$ asserts \emph{generic exact finite-sample identifiability} --- uniqueness of $(a,T)$ up to gauge --- not a conditioning bound and not an algorithmic recovery guarantee; stability near the thresholds is the separate third notion of Sec.~2, quantified in Secs.~6 and 8.

The picture is a dichotomy, and it sets up the rest of the paper. When $N\ge K+p$, overdetermination \emph{alone} separates gain from object --- no statistical mechanism is logically needed, though randomization still buys stable conditioning (the identifiability axis of Fig.~1). When $K<N<K+p$, tallness is insufficient even generically, and the stationarity anchor of Sec.~5 (or a support/sparsity prior) becomes \emph{required}, not merely convenient. The two regimes meet through the drift bandwidth: a gain occupying a fraction $\rho_{\mathrm{bw}}$ of the frame band spans $p\approx \rho_{\mathrm{bw}}N$ low-pass modes, so $N\ge K+p$ reads as

\[ N \;\gtrsim\; \frac{K}{1-\rho_{\mathrm{bw}}}. \]

Slow drift ($\rho_{\mathrm{bw}}\ll1$) is cured by modest oversampling; as $\rho_{\mathrm{bw}}\to1$, $p\to N$ and blind algebraic separation is impossible for \emph{any} $N$, forcing the statistical route.

\section{Statistical identifiability: the stationarity anchor}

Section~4 offered one escape from the gain--object orbit: buy identifiability with surplus rows. This section develops a second, logically distinct escape that operates even at fixed $N$ and, crucially, explains \emph{why} randomized acquisition self-calibrates while ordered acquisition does not. The idea is best stated before the mathematics. A drifting gain is a boat carried by a slow current, and the bucket record is our only view of the water. If the waves change character with position --- as the ordered Hadamard coefficient sequence does, sweeping through the object's spectral envelope --- we cannot tell the current from the coastline: a slow change in the record may be drift, or may be the scenery. But if the waves are \emph{statistically identical everywhere}, any slow modulation of the record has exactly one available explanation. Local averaging then drops an anchor into the distribution itself: it pins the \emph{relative} drift, while the absolute scale remains gauged away. This intuition, and nothing stronger, is what the results below formalize.

The log-domain record can be described by $Y_n=\log R_n=\ell_n+\log B_n$. The anchor requirement is a condition on the carrier alone:

\begin{nameddef}{Condition ($\star$) (stationary carrier, quantitative form)}
There is a scalar $m_T$ --- depending on the object but not on time, and absorbed by the global-scale gauge --- such that the windowed carrier averages over windows $W_n$ of length $W$ satisfy
\[\max_n \Big| W^{-1}\!\!\sum_{k\in W_n}\!\log B_k \;-\; m_T \Big| \;\longrightarrow\; 0 \quad \text{in probability as } W\to\infty,\ W/N\to 0 .\]
The condition presupposes a positive carrier on the analyzed record --- physically, a positivity/offset margin, or the soft-log $\psi_\alpha$ of Sec.~6 in the photon-counting case.
\end{nameddef}

The displayed criterion is deliberately \emph{uniform} --- a maximum over all window locations; pointwise consistency at a single $n$ is strictly weaker, and upgrading it to the displayed uniform control over the $\sim N$ window locations typically costs a $\log N$ / high-probability refinement (the $\delta\mapsto\delta/N$ step of Appendix~C).

I.i.d. random illumination satisfies ($\star$) by construction. For a finite random row permutation of a deterministic pattern set, or a single fixed SRHT draw, ($\star$) is \emph{not} automatic: a permuted record is exchangeable --- which anchors windowed averages in distribution --- but a particular draw can still fail for a spectrally concentrated object; the quantitative version of this distinction is the permutation bound of Sec.~7, and the permuted-arm rejections in Fig.~2 are its visible trace. For the i.i.d. case:

\begin{namedthm}{Proposition 1 (random-basis carrier)}
For i.i.d. random patterns with pixel mean $\mu_I$ and standard deviation $\sigma_I$, the carrier $\{B_n\}$ is white and strictly stationary, and the object enters its first two moments only through the scalars $S_1=\sum_j T_j$ and $S_2=\sum_j T_j^2$: $\mathbb{E}B_n=\mu_I S_1$, $\operatorname{Var}B_n=\sigma_I^2 S_2$, hence $\mathrm{CV}_B=(\sigma_I/\mu_I)/\sqrt{K_{\mathrm{eff}}}$ with $K_{\mathrm{eff}}=S_1^2/S_2$. Gaussian and log-domain approximations additionally require the Lindeberg-type spikiness condition $K_\infty=S_2/\|T\|_\infty^2\to\infty$; the reduction of the object to two scalars holds at this Gaussian-approximation level, not exactly. Higher cumulants and the log-domain mean can depend on the object's shape beyond $(S_1,S_2)$, but for an i.i.d. carrier every such term is time-independent and shifts only the gauge scalar $m_T$; it does not affect relative-gain recovery. (Proof: Appendix~C.)
\end{namedthm}

Figure~2 shows the two carriers side by side: the random trace is a flat, object-independent noise band, whereas the ordered Hadamard trace \emph{is} the object's coefficient envelope --- a deterministic, strongly non-stationary signature imprinted on acquisition time. Under ordered scanning the object masquerades as a temporal trend, precisely the degree of freedom a slow gain occupies, and ($\star$) fails. We emphasize the correct logical status of the condition:

\begin{namedthm}{Proposition 2 (($\star$) is an estimator criterion, not a universal one)}
Condition ($\star$) is necessary and sufficient for consistency of the blind windowed (stationarity-anchor) gain correction. It is \emph{not} an if-and-only-if condition for identifiability by all mechanisms: the overdetermined designs of Sec.~4 identify algebraically without any stationarity, and conversely ($\star$) confers no exact algebraic identifiability. (Proof: Appendix~C.)
\end{namedthm}

\noindent\textbf{Bucket conventions.} Three bucket-signal conventions coexist in this paper, and the log-domain results apply to them differently. (i) \emph{Signed coefficient model:} $B_n$ is a signed transform coefficient (e.g.\ a bare Walsh--Hadamard coefficient); it can vanish or change sign, so $\log B_n$ is undefined and no log-domain gain estimator is available without further structure. (ii) \emph{Physical nonnegative offset model:} the optical bucket is a nonnegative intensity; signed patterns are realized with a bias/offset so that $B_n\ge B_{\min}>0$. Theorem~B and the estimators of Sec.~6 are stated in this model (assumption (iv) below). (iii) \emph{$\pm$ pairwise model:} each signed pattern is acquired as a complementary pair of nonnegative patterns whose buckets are differenced; the difference is signed again, but the drift now enters through adjacent-pair increments controlled by $\rho_{\mathrm{pair}}$ (Sec.~8). The raw signed arms of Sec.~9, which feed bare Walsh coefficients to the gain estimator, follow convention (i) and are \emph{diagnostic only}: they visualize the chronology failure but are not a valid physical log-domain estimator without the offset of (ii) or the pairing of (iii).

The main statistical result is then:

\begin{namedthm}{Theorem B (statistical relative-gain identifiability)}
Assume the log-domain model
\[Y_n=\log R_n=\ell_n+m_T+z_n,\]
where (i) $m_T=\mathbb{E}\log B_n$ is a scalar depending on the object but not on time; (ii) the carrier fluctuations $\{z_n\}$ are centered, strictly stationary, and weakly dependent with summable autocovariances, $\sigma_{\mathrm{abs}}^2=\sum_{h}|\gamma(h)|<\infty$ where $\gamma(h)=\operatorname{Cov}(z_0,z_h)$ ($\beta$-mixing with summable coefficients is sufficient) --- the \emph{signed long-run variance} $\sigma_{\mathrm{LR}}^2=\gamma(0)+2\sum_{h\ge1}\gamma(h)\ (\le\sigma_{\mathrm{abs}}^2)$ then also exists and is the asymptotic variance of window means; the two constants play distinct roles below; (iii) the log-gain $\ell\in S$ lies in a normalized-time H\"older-$\beta$ class, $|\ell_j-\ell_k|\le L\,(|j-k|/N)^{\beta}$; and (iv) $R_n>0$ on the analyzed record so the logarithm is defined (positivity/offset margin; the soft-log of Sec.~6 covers the photon-counting case). Then, for windows satisfying \textbf{both} $W\to\infty$ \textbf{and} $W/N\to 0$, windowed averages of $Y_n$ estimate $\ell_n+m_T$ consistently, with non-asymptotic stochastic error $O_P(\sigma_{\mathrm{abs}}/\sqrt{W})$ --- the asymptotic variance of the window mean being $\sigma_{\mathrm{LR}}^2/W$ --- and windowed drift bias $O(L\,(W/N)^{\beta})$; consequently the \emph{centered} log-gain $\ell_n-\overline{\ell}$ --- the relative gain, up to one global scale --- is identifiable and consistently estimable. The scalar $m_T$, and with it the absolute gauge of $\ell$, is not recoverable. (Proof: Appendix~C.)
\end{namedthm}

Theorem~B must be read with its scope intact. It is an asymptotic, high-probability statement about \emph{relative}-gain recovery up to the global-scale gauge --- never exact finite-sample identifiability of $(a,T)$. In particular it does not contradict Theorem~A: a random square design remains algebraically non-identifiable; what randomness buys is statistical separability, a different currency. This is also what distinguishes the mechanism from the bilinear-inverse identifiability literature \cite{LiLeeBresler1501.06120,KechKrahmer1603.07316,ChoudharyMitra1402.2637}: the anchor is a property of the \emph{temporal distribution} of the carrier, not of subspace injectivity.

The anchor also explains why the self-calibrating gain corrections of Refs.~\cite{PengChen2024APL,PengXiaoChen2025OE} can work at all. Under Proposition~1's Gaussian approximation the bucket record given $(a,\mu_B,\sigma_B)$ is a slow trend plus stationary residuals, and an SCGI-style Gaussian-likelihood fit of that trend is a maximum-likelihood realization of the windowed anchor estimator, inheriting Theorem~B's consistency guarantee (and its gauge).

Figure~3 is the core numerical evidence. Across a panel of objects, the blind gain-estimation error $\|\hat a-a\|/\|a\|$ under random illumination tracks $\mathrm{CV}_B/\sqrt{W}$ object by object --- to leading Gaussian/log-variance order, and within the tested object panel, the object enters only through $K_{\mathrm{eff}}$ --- and once normalized by that floor the curves collapse across objects to within $\pm5$--$7\%$; under ordered Hadamard the curves scatter by object and do not decay with $W$ --- the object envelope is a bias no amount of averaging removes. The window $W$ itself carries a bias--variance trade-off (drift curvature versus $\mathrm{CV}_B^2/W$), whose optimal resolution and minimax rate are the subject of Sec.~6.

\section{Estimation rate, lower bounds, and low-photon robustness}

Section~5 establishes that relative gain is \emph{identifiable} from a stationary carrier; here we show it is recovered \emph{efficiently and predictably}. The baseline estimator is the plain windowed log of Theorem~B: average $Y_n=\log R_n$ over a window $W$ of frames sharing a slowly varying gain. For photon-counting records we work with its robust generalization, the windowed \emph{soft-log}: average $\psi_\alpha(c)=\log(c+\alpha)$ and invert the calibration curve, $\hat\theta_n=m_\alpha^{-1}\!\big(\mathrm{mean}_W\,\psi_\alpha\big)$, where $m_\alpha(\theta)=\mathbb{E}[\psi_\alpha(C)]$ denotes the calibration curve for $C\sim\mathrm{Pois}(\Lambda_0 e^{\theta} B+d)$. As $\alpha\to0$ at high mean count $\bar\lambda$ the calibration curve becomes the identity and the soft-log reduces to the plain windowed log estimator; the rate theorem below is stated for the general (soft-log) estimator and covers both regimes. Because the log-gain $\ell$ lies in a H\"older-$\beta$ class of smooth drift (notation warning: $\beta$ is reserved for H\"older smoothness and $\alpha$ for the soft-log offset throughout; the two symbols are unrelated and must not be conflated), the window trades bias against variance in the usual nonparametric way: wider $W$ suppresses variance as $O(1/W)$ but incurs an approximation bias set by the local smoothness $\beta$ and the drift Lipschitz constant $L_a$ (the per-frame form of Theorem~B's normalized-time constant, $L_a=L\,N^{-\beta}$).

\begin{namedthm}{Theorem C (windowed rate)}
For log-gain in a H\"older-$\beta$ class with constant $L_a$, the bias-optimized windowed estimator attains
\[\mathrm{MSE}^{*}\;\le\; C\,\kappa_{\min}^{-2}\,\big[\kappa_{\max}L_a\big]^{2/(2\beta+1)}\,\sigma_{\alpha,\mathrm{LR}}^{4\beta/(2\beta+1)},\]
the standard nonparametric rate in the effective per-frame noise $\sigma_{\alpha,\mathrm{LR}}$. (Proof: Appendix~D.)
\end{namedthm}

This rate is not merely achievable but \emph{optimal}. An Assouad two-point construction over the same smoothness class (a van Trees / Pinsker argument in the Sobolev variant) produces a matching lower bound, so no estimator improves on Theorem~C by more than constants.

\begin{namedthm}{Theorem D (minimax optimality)}
Over the H\"older-$\beta$ drift class the windowed estimator is minimax-rate optimal: the lower bound matches Theorem~C at the level of rates. Sharp minimax constants remain open. (Proof: Appendix~D.)
\end{namedthm}

The floor beneath both bounds is Fisher-information geometry. Working in the gauge quotient (global scale is unobservable, Sec.~5), the relevant object is the \emph{quotient Fisher information}: the Fisher matrix restricted to directions transverse to the scale orbit. This makes the identifiability dichotomy quantitative --- the Fisher matrix is singular exactly along the null direction of Theorem~A. Non-identifiability is not poor conditioning; it is a genuine flat direction of the likelihood, the ambiguity $(a,T)\mapsto(a s^{-1}, sT)$ made infinitesimal.

Which design constant controls what is a spikiness question, and three distinct functionals appear. $K_{\mathrm{eff}}=S_1^2/S_2$ (Prop~1) governs excitation and the pairwise-Hadamard residual-gain budget; $K_\infty=S_2/\|T\|_\infty^2$ is the Lindeberg spikiness parameter gating the Gaussian and log-domain approximations (Prop~1) and the windowed-energy concentration; $K_4=S_2^2/\sum_j T_j^4$ enters the concentration guarantee for permutation whitening (Sec.~7, via $\min(\epsilon^2 K_4,\epsilon K_\infty)\ge C\log(K/\delta)$). Conflating them mislabels the operative bottleneck: a design flat in $K_{\mathrm{eff}}$ can still be spiky in $K_\infty$.

Low photon counts do not break the log domain. The Poisson derivative identity $\frac{d}{d\lambda}\mathbb{E}f(C)=\mathbb{E}[f(C{+}1){-}f(C)]$ gives a sensitivity $\kappa_\alpha(\lambda)\to 1$ at high $\bar\lambda$ and $\kappa_\alpha(\lambda)\to\lambda\log(1+1/\alpha)$ as $\bar\lambda\to 0$; the offset $\alpha$ absorbs empty bins so no frame ever contributes $\log 0$. Propagating through Theorem~C, the photon-starved variance degrades gracefully as $\sim 1/(W\bar\lambda)$ --- the Fisher-matched scaling, since the Poisson information for log-intensity is $\lambda$ itself. Zero photons carry zero Fisher information; they cut precision but never diverge (Fig.~7).

The caveat is stated honestly. At $\bar\lambda<1$ the soft-log becomes shrinkage-bias-dominated: $\psi_\alpha$ pulls the estimate toward $\log\alpha$, and the estimator sits \emph{below} its unbiased (local) Fisher reference not because it beats the bound but because bias is present. The $1/(W\bar\lambda)$ law is the variance-limited regime above this crossover; below it, honest accounting reports the bias floor rather than the optimistic Fisher curve, and recovering the rate requires the offset-design or full Poisson-mixture MLE variants.

\section{Basis design: randomized orthogonal acquisition (SRHT)}

A reader who has followed the stationarity anchor of Sec.~5 will raise a natural objection: the anchor was purchased with random illumination, yet the appeal of ordered Hadamard was its exact, perfectly conditioned inverse. Must one surrender orthogonal conditioning to buy self-calibration? The answer is no. The randomized Hadamard transform --- an orthogonal Hadamard carrier composed with a random sign flip and a random pixel permutation, known in the sketching literature as the SRHT (subsampled randomized Hadamard transform) --- keeps a full-rank, well-conditioned forward map while randomizing the \emph{temporal handwriting} of the object. One shuffles the deck without bending the cards. We use the \emph{full} $N=K$ randomized transform throughout; ``subsampled'' is reserved for the $N<K$ case, which additionally raises a bias/prior question outside our scope.

The mechanism is exact. Writing the per-coefficient excitation as $Z_g$ under a random diagonal sign $D$, the residual gain--object coupling is governed entirely by the Walsh spectrum of the squared object. With $w = T^2$,
\[\operatorname{Cov}_D(Z_g, Z_h) = \hat{w}(g+h),\]
the Walsh transform of $w$ evaluated at the dyadic sum of indices. Sign randomization alone therefore whitens the excitation --- decorrelates every coefficient pair --- \textbf{if and only if} $w$ is Walsh-flat, i.e.\ $\hat{w}(k)$ is negligible for all $k\neq 0$. This is a condition on the object, not the design: a structured $T$ concentrates $\hat{w}$ off the origin and sign flips cannot remove it.

The random permutation supplies the missing genericity. It acts as a probabilistic Walsh-flattener: permuting pixels spreads the mass of $\hat{w}$ across the spectrum, and the off-origin covariance is driven below tolerance $\varepsilon$ with probability $1-\delta$ once the object is spectrally spread enough,
\[\min\!\big(\varepsilon^2 K_4,\ \varepsilon K_\infty\big) \ \geq\ C \log(K/\delta),\]
with $K_4$ and $K_\infty$ the spread functionals of Sec.~6. Both diverge as the object's energy delocalizes and collapse when a few pixels dominate; the bound quantifies precisely how much delocalization the permutation needs.

Permutation is a genuine second ingredient, not a substitute for the analysis. Under permutation alone,
\[\operatorname{Var}_P Z_g = \frac{K S_2 - S_1^2}{K-1} = S_2\,\frac{K - K_{\mathrm{eff}}}{K-1},\]
so the residual carries an $O(1/K_{\mathrm{eff}})$ \emph{upper} bound on variance --- reassuring on average --- but no matching lower bound. A flat object realizes the gap: it produces zero excitation, so permutation cannot guarantee two-sided control coefficient by coefficient. The whitening claim is one-sided unless the sign flip and the spectral-spread condition are both in force. A row-order shuffle of the acquisition sequence is a third, weaker operation: it makes the temporal carrier exchangeable --- enough for the windowed anchor of Sec.~5 to hold in distribution --- but it does not by itself guarantee two-sided per-coefficient excitation. Figure~6 isolates these contributions by ablation (none / permutation / sign / both), and the two-sided regime is exactly the ``both'' cell.

This yields a design rule rather than a single number.

\begin{nameddef}{Design rule (blind slow gain)}
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*),leftmargin=*]
\item Do \emph{not} acquire in ordered Hadamard when the nuisance is a blind, slowly drifting gain --- the ordering hands the object a temporal signature (Sec.~4).
\item Randomize the carrier: apply the SRHT sign flip \emph{and} permutation.
\item Verify Walsh-flatness of the realized (permuted) $T^2$ --- the covariance-whitening guarantee is the sign-randomization statement applied after the permutation --- or rely on the permutation bound above with $K_4, K_\infty$ estimated from the object class.
\item Keep a positivity / offset margin so the log or soft-log stage of Sec.~6 stays away from its singularity.
\item Choose the averaging window $W$ from the bias--variance trade of Sec.~6, not from conditioning.
\end{enumerate}
\end{nameddef}

The rule prescribes \emph{both} randomizations because that is the configuration with a two-sided theorem guarantee under the stated spectral-spread condition. The ablation of Fig.~6 reports a different, empirical fact: in the tested panel, either randomization alone already recovered essentially the full advantage. The two statements must not be merged --- the theorem guarantees whitening under its hypotheses, while the ablation shows that, for these objects and this drift, any tested way of breaking the chronology sufficed; we do not promote the panel result to a universal claim.

The synthesis is that orthogonality and self-calibration are compatible: SRHT preserves the $B_L = 1$ leverage of a full orthogonal inversion (Sec.~8) while --- under Walsh-flatness of $T^2$ or a successful permutation of a sufficiently spread object --- making the object carrier statistically stationary in time, so that the only non-stationary structure left in the bucket record is the gain itself. The conditioning that made ordered Hadamard attractive is retained; only its chronology is discarded.

(Proof of the Walsh identity and permutation bound: Appendix~E.)

\section{From identifiability to images: the reconstruction bridge and phase diagram}

Sections~4--7 answer the question a statistician asks --- \emph{is the gain identifiable, and at what rate?} --- but an imaging practitioner asks a different one: \emph{how many gray levels do I lose?} This section supplies the bridge. The result is exact, holds at finite $N$ and finite photon count, and reduces every acquisition strategy in this paper to three numbers that can be read off a single chart before any hardware is built.

The corrected bucket record can be described by $z_n=(1+\delta_n)b_n+\xi_n$ with $b=AT$, where $\delta_n$ denotes the \emph{residual} multiplicative gain after whatever blind correction the pipeline applied; let $\hat T=Lz$ be any linear reconstruction.

\begin{namedthm}{Theorem 1 (master finite-noise relMSE identity)}
Assume the residual gain $\delta$ and the additive noise $\xi$ are uncorrelated, $C_{\delta\xi}:=\operatorname{Cov}(\delta,\xi)=0$. Then, without Gaussian or small-noise approximation,
\[\frac{\mathbb{E}\|\hat T-T\|^2}{S_2}\;=\;\underbrace{\frac{\|(LA-I)T+L\,\mathrm{diag}(b)\,m_\delta\|^2}{S_2}}_{\text{bias}}\;+\;\underbrace{\frac{\operatorname{tr}\!\big(L\,\mathrm{diag}(b)\,V_\delta\,\mathrm{diag}(b)\,L^\top\big)}{S_2}}_{\text{residual gain}}\;+\;\underbrace{\frac{\operatorname{tr}\!\big(L\,\Sigma_\xi L^\top\big)}{S_2}}_{\text{read + Poisson}}.\]
If the residual gains are independent with $\mathrm{Var}\,\delta_n=v$, the gain term collapses to $v\,B_L$ with the \textbf{leverage} $B_L=(1/S_2)\sum_n b_n^2\|Le_n\|^2$; coherent (smooth) residuals require the full matrix form. Without the uncorrelatedness hypothesis the identity acquires exactly one additional term, $(2/S_2)\,\operatorname{tr}\!\big[L\,\mathrm{diag}(b)\,C_{\delta\xi}\,L^{\top}\big]$; it vanishes under the stated hypothesis, but \emph{not} automatically when the gain estimate $\hat a$ is computed from the same noisy record that carries $\xi$ (same-record dependence). (Proof: Appendix~F.)
\end{namedthm}

The Poisson plug-in $\Sigma_\xi=\mathrm{diag}(\tau_{G,n}^2+b_n^2/\Phi_n)$ is exact at all photon counts \emph{after the post-calibration substitution $\hat a_n\to a_n$}; before that substitution both noise variances carry the factor $(a_n/\hat a_n)^2$ (Appendix~F). With that qualifier, the identity --- not an approximation to it --- is what Fig.~4 tests. One metric convention matters when reading measured numbers: the identity is stated for the raw error $\mathbb{E}\|\hat T-T\|^2/S_2$, whereas the relMSE \emph{measured} in Figs.~4--5 is computed after least-squares scale alignment of $\hat T$ to $T$ (the gauge convention of Sec.~2); alignment can pull $\mathrm{relMSE}/v$ below 1 for DC-dominated objects by absorbing the gain component co-linear with the object (Sec.~9). The two roles are kept separate in what follows: the \emph{raw} (un-aligned) identity is verified directly by the injected coherent-residual experiment of Sec.~9 (E4, supplementary panels), while Fig.~4 reads the scale-aligned image metric. Specializing $L$:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Orthogonal / SRHT full inversion:} $B_L=1$, so $\mathrm{relMSE}=v+(1/S_2)\sum_n(\tau_{G,n}^2+b_n^2/\Phi_n)$. The imager passes residual gain through at unit leverage --- no amplification, no dilution.
\item \textbf{Pairwise (differenced) Hadamard:} the gain term is $R_{\mathrm{pair}} = (K_{\mathrm{eff}}/4)\,D_H(T)\,\mathrm{Var}(\Delta)$, with $D_H\approx 1$ for non-DC coefficients and $\mathrm{Var}(\Delta)\approx 2s^2\,r(\rho_{\mathrm{pair}})$ the adjacent-pair gain increment, where $r(\cdot)$ denotes the adjacent-pair decorrelation function of the drift --- $r(\rho)=1-e^{-\rho}$ for the OU drift of Sec.~9, with $r(\rho)\approx\rho$ for small $\rho$. Differencing converts \emph{slowness} of the drift directly into suppression.
\item \textbf{Random / DGI:} $\mathrm{relMSE}_{\mathrm{DGI}} = C_0(\mathrm{pipeline})/N + (1+C_0(\mathrm{pipeline}))v/N + $ noise terms, where $C_0(\mathrm{pipeline})=\mathbb{E}\|Z\|^2/S_2-1$ denotes the exact one-sample constant of the correlator ($C_0=K+\beta_4-2$ for symmetric zero-mean patterns). There is \textbf{no universal $C_0$} --- hence the argument: it depends on the pattern statistics, background, and normalization, and must be measured from the pipeline at hand --- for our SCGI-style testbed \cite{PengChen2024APL,PengXiaoChen2025OE} the measured value is used throughout Figs.~4 and 5.
\end{itemize}

This yields an honest corollary that the identifiability results alone would obscure: \textbf{at equal residual variance $v$, orthogonal inversion is the best conduit} --- it has $B_L=1$ and no $C_0(\mathrm{pipeline})/N$ estimator floor. The randomized bases do not win because they transmit gain error more gracefully; they win because Theorem~B and the window estimator of Sec.~6 let $v$ \emph{itself} be driven down blindly, at rate $1/W$ per window, whereas an ordered scan offers no blind handle on $v$ at all.

The competition between these closed forms produces a finite-$N$ flip boundary.

\begin{namedthm}{Prop 3 (finite-$N$ flip boundary)}
$\rho^{*}=\inf\{\rho_{\mathrm{pair}}: R_{\mathrm{pair}}(\rho_{\mathrm{pair}})\ge R_{\mathrm{rand}}(\rho_{\mathrm{pair}},N)\}$ satisfies, in the small-drift regime,
\[r(\rho^{*})=\frac{2\big[C_0(\mathrm{pipeline})/N+(1+C_0(\mathrm{pipeline}))v_{\mathrm{blind}}/N+R_{\mathrm{DGI,noise}}-R_{\mathrm{pair,noise}}\big]}{K_{\mathrm{eff}}\,D_H\,s^{2}},\]
whose leading order ($r(\rho)=\rho$, $D_H=1$, negligible noise and $v_{\mathrm{blind}}$) is the heuristic $\rho^{*}\approx 2C_0(\mathrm{pipeline})/(N K_{\mathrm{eff}} s^{2})$. (Proof: Appendix~F.)
\end{namedthm}

\begin{nameddef}{Remark (two decorrelation conventions --- do not conflate)}
Throughout, $\rho_{\mathrm{pair}}$ denotes \emph{adjacent-pair} gain decorrelation, the variable entering $\mathrm{Var}(\Delta)$ and Prop~3; $\rho_{\mathrm{bw}}$ denotes the normalized gain \emph{bandwidth}, $p\approx\rho_{\mathrm{bw}}N$, the variable entering the tall-design thresholds of Sec.~4 ($N\gtrsim K/(1-\rho_{\mathrm{bw}})$). Both grow with drift speed but are not equal, and every axis in Fig.~5 is labeled by which one it carries.
\end{nameddef}

Figure~4 shows the bridge validated term by term: predicted bias, gain, and noise contributions against measured relMSE across bases, drift speeds, and photon budgets --- numerical evidence, on simulation, that Theorem~1 is an accounting identity rather than a bound. Figure~5 assembles the payoff: the phase diagram over (basis, $\rho_{\mathrm{pair}}$, noise level $\sigma$) with the $\rho^{*}$ curve overlaid. Three regimes emerge. For \textbf{slow drift} ($\rho_{\mathrm{pair}}<\rho^{*}$), pairwise Hadamard is fine: differencing suppresses what little the gain moves, and orthogonal conditioning is kept for free. For \textbf{intermediate drift}, randomized carriers win --- the stationarity anchor buys a small $v_{\mathrm{blind}}$ that no ordered scan can reach; the above-floor winner is the SRHT-paired pipeline, which keeps orthogonal conditioning, not bare i.i.d.-random/DGI, which pays its $C_0(\mathrm{pipeline})/N$ estimator floor in absolute error. For \textbf{fast drift} ($\rho_{\mathrm{bw}}\to 1$, $p$ approaching $N$), every \emph{blind algebraic} strategy hits the noise floor together --- consistent with the information-loss intuition behind Theorem~A$'$'s counting; this is a statement about blind separation, not about the physics --- an externally calibrated or metered gain restores the problem to ordinary inversion. The chart is a pre-acquisition instrument: locate your drift spectrum and photon budget on it, and the go/no-go decision --- and the basis --- follow before the first frame is taken.

\section{Numerical experiments}

The theory of Secs.~4--8 makes quantitative predictions --- a stationarity dichotomy, a $W^{-1/2}$ estimation law whose object dependence is confined to $K_{\mathrm{eff}}$, an exact relMSE identity, a flip boundary, and a photon-limited Fisher scaling --- and this section tests each of them in a series of simulations. A closing subsection integrates five verification experiments completed after the main panel: the tall-design rank threshold (Fig.~8), permutation-whitening power, the coherent-residual validation of Theorem~1, the $C_0$ audit, and oracle-to-blind baselines (supplementary panels).

\noindent\textbf{Protocol.} All results are simulations; no hardware data enter, consistent with the scope of Sec.~1. Comparisons are made under matched acquisition budgets --- equal frame counts and equal total flux for every arm --- so that no basis is granted extra photons or extra rows. The drift is an Ornstein--Uhlenbeck log-gain trace with per-frame decorrelation rate $\rho$, the knob that sets the adjacent-pair decorrelation $\rho_{\mathrm{pair}}$ of Sec.~8; within each seed a single trace is shared by every measurement arm, so the bases face the identical nuisance realization rather than their own luck, while the traces differ across seeds (drift standard deviation 0.030--0.104). Blind correction uses a single estimator for all arms --- the sliding-window mean automatic gain control (AGC) realization of the proposed windowed estimator of Sec.~6 --- with no per-arm tuning and no denoising or post-processing anywhere in the pipeline. The object panel holds ten objects spanning $K_{\mathrm{eff}}=S_1^2/S_2$ from 60 to 864, each run at five drift seeds (fifteen for the reconstruction bridge), with errors computed against ground truth on the object support mask after the least-squares scale alignment of Sec.~8. Concretely: the object panel comprises ten shared objects from a fixed generator --- two synthetic digits, the letters A and L, a vertical stripe (width $\approx$ image/8), an annulus (radii $\approx$ 0.22/0.34 of the image span), and four procedural natural patches --- each normalized to $[0,1]$ and spanning $K_{\mathrm{eff}}=59.8$--$864.3$; every experiment below shares base seed $20240708$. \noindent\textbf{Carrier stationarity (Fig.~2).} This experiment applies a \emph{variance-stationarity diagnostic} to the noiseless carrier $\{B_n\}$, before any gain is applied, at 8192 frames per arm on $64\times64$ objects; the Brown--Forsythe test checks chunk-variance equality --- a consequence of the stationarity that ($\star$) requires, not a direct test of ($\star$)'s windowed log-carrier means. As shown in Fig.~2, the Brown--Forsythe variance-stationarity test at $p<10^{-3}$ rejects 7 of 10 objects for ordered Hadamard, with $p$ reaching $10^{-20}$, against 0 of 10 for both i.i.d.\ random ensembles. (These are uncorrected per-test $p$-values; under a multiplicity correction across the object $\times$ arm panel --- e.g.\ Bonferroni --- the strongest rejections clear the adjusted threshold by many orders of magnitude, while rejections near the $p<10^{-3}$ gate should be read with that correction in mind.) The permuted arms (randomly permuted paired Hadamard and SRHT) each reject 2 of 10: a \emph{fixed} permutation does not Walsh-flatten every object's $T^2$ spectrum --- the permutation whitening of Sec.~7 is a probabilistic statement over permutations, and an object with concentrated spectral energy can alias against a particular draw. This residue is the theory's own prediction, not an anomaly. The Parseval invariant closes the loop: every exhaustively paired arm has carrier $\mathrm{CV}_B = 1/\sqrt{K_{\mathrm{eff}}}$ to a relative accuracy of about $10^{-6}$, and the random arms match $(\sigma_I/\mu_I)/\sqrt{K_{\mathrm{eff}}}$ within $\pm2\%$ --- so the ordered pathology lives entirely in the temporal variance structure, not in the marginal spread of the carrier. The rejection proportions are descriptive counts over this ten-object panel (no resampled intervals).

\noindent\textbf{Blind gain identifiability (Fig.~3).} This is the core evidence for Theorem~B, run over seven arms including the raw, non-paired Hadamard arms that feed bare signed Walsh coefficients to the estimator --- convention (i) of the bucket conventions of Sec.~5, a \emph{diagnostic-only} configuration that is not a valid physical log-domain estimator without offset or pairing. As shown in Fig.~3, the chronology failure of Theorem~A is made visible: the raw ordered arm's blind gain error is $\approx 0.96$ and does not decay with the window ($0.95$ at $W=4$, still $0.90$ at $W=512$). Shuffling the raw frames improves this to $\approx 0.86$ --- permutation helps even here --- but the arm remains roughly $40\times$ worse than the random and paired arms at $\approx 0.02$, because without pairing the near-zero-mean carrier leaves the AGC ill-posed. Within the variance-obeying arms, the slow-drift variance-segment slopes of $\log(\mathrm{err})$ against $\log W$ are $-0.39$ (random binary), $-0.60$ (SRHT), and $-0.61$ (randomly permuted paired Hadamard) --- all inside the $W^{-1/2}$ band --- while naturally ordered paired Hadamard is shallow at $-0.24$: differencing cancels the drift within pairs but leaves the ordered envelope. Normalizing by the theory floor $\sqrt{\mathrm{CV}_B^2/W}$ collapses the panel: the paired arms sit at $0.40$--$0.41$ and the random arms at $0.89$--$0.90$, each bundle collapsing across all ten objects to within $\pm5$--$7\%$, and the best-window error correlates with $K_{\mathrm{eff}}$ at $-0.86$ to $-0.90$ (descriptive correlations over the ten-object panel; no resampled intervals) --- consistent, within this tested panel, with the object entering through $K_{\mathrm{eff}}$ alone, as Proposition~1 predicts to leading Gaussian/log-variance order. The shuffled-paired arm performing like the random arms bears out the permutation analysis of Sec.~7 end to end on this panel: pairing cancels the slow drift within pairs, and the permutation restores the stationarity that the ordering destroyed.

The 20-seed uncertainty replication (24{,}400 rows total) confirms these slopes under the fixed $W\le16$ variance-segment convention: median log--log slope with 95\% bootstrap CI is $-0.41$ $[-0.42,-0.41]$ for random binary, $-0.39$ $[-0.40,-0.37]$ for random uniform, $-0.58$ $[-0.60,-0.54]$ for SRHT-paired, and $-0.62$ $[-0.64,-0.61]$ for randomly permuted paired Hadamard, while the naturally ordered paired arm sits at $+0.03$ $[-0.01,+0.04]$ --- a CI straddling zero, i.e.\ no detectable decay --- and the raw ordered arm's error is $0.958$ $[0.956,0.959]$, essentially flat. The corresponding best-window blind gain errors at this replication's operating point ($\rho_{\mathrm{pair}}=10^{-3}$, $s=0.1$) are reported in Table~1. It is worth noting that this replication's slope fits use the fixed $W\le16$ variance-segment convention, whereas the exploratory slopes and $R^2$ figures above used an adaptive segment; the two conventions agree on every qualitative conclusion.

\noindent\textbf{The reconstruction bridge (Fig.~4).} This experiment tests Theorem~1 as an identity rather than a bound, on a fully balanced 8100-row design: three reconstructors, three transform sizes $N\in\{1024,2048,4096\}$, six injected residual-gain variances $v$, ten objects, fifteen seeds. It can be observed that the deterministic inverses are flat in $N$, as $B_L=1$ demands: the per-$v$ log--log slopes have maximal magnitude $0.074$ (orthogonal) and $0.014$ (SRHT) --- no $1/N$ reconstruction floor. The random/DGI arm decays as $1/N$ at every $v$ (per-$v$ slopes $-1.031$ to $-0.974$), and its leverage law is quantitative: the measured relMSE agrees with $\mathrm{leverage}\times v$ within $[0.91, 1.06]$, with $\mathrm{leverage}\times N$ between $7.38\times10^{5}$ and $7.68\times10^{5}$ across the $N$ sweep (a $4\%$ drift). For the orthogonal inverse, $\mathrm{relMSE}/v \approx 1.0$ for 8 of 10 objects; the two DC-dominated objects --- natural\_patch at $0.35$ and stripe at $0.54$ --- fall below the $v$ line because the scale alignment of the metric absorbs the gain component co-linear with the object, an alignment effect rather than a failure of the identity. Fig.~4's numbers are accordingly read in the \emph{scale-aligned image metric}; the raw-MSE identity-verification role is carried by the injected-residual E4 comparison below.

\noindent\textbf{Phase diagram and ablation (Figs.~5 and 6).} The phase diagram sweeps the drift rate $\rho\in[10^{-3},10]$ and noise level $\sigma\in[0.05,0.5]$ at equal frames, with an above-floor gate $\mathrm{relMSE}<0.5$ separating cells where reconstruction is meaningful from cells at the noise floor --- the gate excludes reconstructions whose error energy is at least half the object energy, a conservative meaningfulness cut; cells failing it are reported as noise floor rather than assigned a winner. It is numerically demonstrated that SRHT-paired acquisition with pairwise correction wins all 28 above-floor cells of the 44-cell winner map; the remaining 16 cells are honestly labeled noise floor rather than assigned a winner, and the flip-boundary fits reach $R^2 = 0.989$--$0.9995$ against the closed form of Prop~3 (winner counts and boundary locations are descriptive results of the specified grid; survival-style intervals are left to future work, Table~1). The ablation isolates the mechanism at the single above-floor drift point $\rho=10^{-3}$: SRHT gains $+5.45$ dB over ordered Hadamard, while sign randomization alone gains $+5.47$ dB and permutation alone $+5.39$ dB --- each ingredient by itself recovers essentially the full advantage, and combining them is not additive; these dB gains are descriptive values at this single grid point (no resampled intervals). What matters is that the chronology is randomized, not the particular form of the randomization. At the largest simulated adjacent-pair decorrelation rates ($\rho\ge1$), all blind variants empirically approach the noise floor within $\pm0.09$ dB; this is consistent with the information-loss intuition behind the $p\to N$ algebraic limit of Theorem~A$'$, but it is not a direct test of the low-pass dimension threshold unless gain bandwidth ($\rho_{\mathrm{bw}}$) and adjacent-pair decorrelation ($\rho_{\mathrm{pair}}$) are controlled separately.

\noindent\textbf{Low-photon robustness (Fig.~7).} This experiment tests the soft-log analysis of Sec.~6 under Poisson counting, on identical random-basis carriers and shared gain traces. As shown in Fig.~7, the soft-log stays finite at every photon budget and, at mean counts $\bar\lambda\le1$, outperforms the naive clipped logarithm by $23$--$46\times$ (the Anscombe transform, separately, by $19.5$--$30\times$). In the variance-limited regime the Fisher-matched $1/(W\bar\lambda)$ law holds: the fitted log--log slope is $-0.910$ over $\bar\lambda\in[2,32]$, shallowing to $-0.730$ over $[1,16]$ as the fit window approaches the $\bar\lambda\sim1$ bias-to-variance transition. Below $\bar\lambda\sim1$ the estimator is shrinkage-bias-dominated, and its MSE sits \emph{below} the unbiased (local) Fisher reference --- because it is biased, not because it beats the bound, exactly the caveat of Sec.~6. (A per-budget decomposition of that MSE into bias and variance contributions below the crossover requires tracking the windowed estimate against the known gain trace per draw and is deferred to a follow-up run; the sub-Fisher MSE together with the calibration-curve shrinkage of Sec.~6 already identifies the regime as bias-dominated.) At the high-photon end the floor is drift-limited rather than photon-limited: the gain MSE of $2.78\times10^{-4}$ at $\rho=10^{-3}$ falls to $1.73\times10^{-4}$ when the drift slows to $\rho=10^{-4}$.

\noindent\textbf{Threshold, whitening-power, and accountability verifications (Fig.~8 and supplementary panels).} A series of five further experiments closes the remaining theory--evidence gaps. Unless a bootstrap interval is stated, every number in this subsection is a descriptive result of its specified grid, with no resampled intervals.

\emph{Tall-design threshold scan (Fig.~8).} A direct test of Theorem~A$'$'s counting at two object sizes: $K=64$ (20 seeds per cell, 1300 cells) and $K=128$ (30 seeds per cell, 1950 cells), each over five low-pass drift dimensions $p\in\{3,5,9,17,33\}$ with frame counts swept through the wall at margins $N-K-p\in[-8,+16]$. As shown in Fig.~8, at both sizes the local rank test on the collision Jacobian transitions from $0\%$ to $100\%$ pass \emph{exactly} at margin $N-K-p=-1$ for every $p$ (3250 cells, no exceptions) --- verifying the \emph{local differential} rank threshold $N=K+p-1$ of Theorem~A$'$; being a rank statement, it verifies the local level and does not by itself test generic-exact uniqueness at $N=K+p$. The companion solver panel (blind alternating recovery, 8 restarts $\times$ 150 iterations) starts at the same wall but climbs slowly --- about $2\%$ success at margin $0$ and only $\sim50\%$ at margin $+16$ for $K=64$, slower still at $K=128$ ($\sim41\%$ at margin $+16$) --- an \emph{algorithmic-recovery} observation, not a uniqueness test: uniqueness and recovery separate exactly as the third notion of Sec.~2 anticipates, with the gap widening as the dimension grows (see also Sec.~10). This exact transition is the empirical support cited for the low-pass three-level hierarchy of Sec.~4 and Appendix~B.

\emph{Multi-permutation whitening power (supplementary panels).} Theorem~E.3's permutation whitening is probabilistic --- over permutations, not per draw --- and this experiment measures that distinction under the Fig.~2 protocol: 32 random pixel permutations $\times$ 10 objects, i.e.\ 320 permuted draws. It can be observed that the Brown--Forsythe rejection rate at $p<10^{-3}$ is $5.9\%$ over the 320 permuted draws, against $70\%$ (7 of 10 objects) for the ordered reference arm. The natural per-draw certificate candidate fails: the maximum non-DC Walsh coefficient of the permuted $T^2$ does \emph{not} predict which draws reject (Spearman $0.092$, $p=0.10$) --- an honest null, consistent with the probabilistic (not per-draw-certifiable) nature of permutation whitening (Sec.~7).

\emph{Coherent-residual validation of Theorem~1 (E4, supplementary panels).} Five injected residual-covariance structures --- i.i.d., AR(1), sinusoidal, low-pass OU, blockwise --- at fixed $v=10^{-3}$, across three reconstructors and ten objects (150 cells), measured in the raw-residual (identity-verification) metric rather than the scale-aligned image metric of Fig.~4. The full matrix law of Theorem~1 tracks the measured relMSE within $4.2\%$ across all 150 cells. The scalar $v\,B_L$ law is exact for the complete orthogonal and SRHT inversions --- for these, $LL^{\top}\propto I/N$ analytically, so the gain term feels only $\mathrm{diag}(V_\delta)$ --- but fails by up to $3.65\times$ for random/DGI under low-pass-coherent residuals: a direct verification of Theorem~1's coherent clause. It is worth noting that E4 validates the matrix law under \emph{injected} residuals; it does not probe the same-record $\delta$--$\xi$ dependence flagged after Theorem~1.

\emph{$C_0$ audit (supplementary panels).} The one-sample constant measured from $2\times10^{4}$ pattern draws per ensemble agrees with the moment formula of Appendix~F to $0.35\%$ mean ($2.01\%$ worst) relative error across four pattern ensembles $\times$ five objects. The strong ensemble dependence --- $O(K)$ for signed Rademacher patterns versus $O(K_{\mathrm{eff}}K)$ for the nonnegative offset ensembles --- confirms that $C_0$ is pipeline-specific, as Theorem~1's DGI specialization insists.

\emph{Oracle, metered, and blind baselines (supplementary panels).} On four held-out objects with OU drift ($\rho=10^{-3}$, $s=0.1$), the accountability ordering static $\ge$ oracle $\ge$ blind-AGC $\ge$ no-correction holds in CNR for 4 of 4 objects, with an oracle$-$blind CNR gap of $0.035$--$0.147$: at this drift point the blind windowed estimator concedes little to a gain oracle, while forgoing correction altogether costs the most.

\section{Discussion: significance, scope, and outlook}

The results of Sections~2--9 are, at bottom, a set of pre-acquisition design tools: quantities one can evaluate on the illumination basis and the expected drift \emph{before} photons are collected, and which predict whether blind gain--object separation will succeed. They are collected here as four deliverables, followed by their limits and the experimental program they invite.

\noindent\textbf{A go/no-go identifiability predictor.} The tall-design thresholds convert ``will this acquisition self-calibrate?'' into arithmetic. For a physical low-pass drift class $S$ of dimension $p=\dim S$, local differential identifiability holds at $N\ge K+p-1$, generic exact finite-sample identifiability at $N\ge K+p$, and uniform (every-object) identifiability at $N\ge 2K+p-1$ (Theorem~A$'$ --- proven at generic-pair level for the physical low-pass drift family, with the uniform-over-drift reading resting on a labeled dimension-count argument plus the exact empirical rank transition of Fig.~8). Written through the bandwidth relation $p\approx\rho_{\mathrm{bw}}N$, the generic threshold becomes $N\gtrsim K/(1-\rho_{\mathrm{bw}})$: slow drift needs only modest oversampling, while $\rho_{\mathrm{bw}}\to1$ ($p\to N$) makes blind algebraic separation impossible for any $N$, forcing reliance on the statistical anchor of Theorem~B --- which itself degrades once the gain decorrelates within the averaging window (the $\rho\ge1$ convergence of Fig.~6). This is a regime map the experimenter reads off before building.

\noindent\textbf{An SRHT design rule.} When blind slow gain is the operative nuisance, do not run ordered Hadamard: its chronological coefficient ordering hands the object a temporal signature indistinguishable from drift. Randomize the carrier instead --- via sign or pixel-permutation randomization of a Walsh--Hadamard design. The two ingredients play distinct roles and should not be compressed into one guarantee: the random \emph{permutation} makes the temporal carrier exchangeable --- the anchor mechanism of Sec.~5 --- while the covariance-whitening guarantee is the \emph{sign-randomization} statement applied after the permutation: sign flips whiten if and only if the realized (permuted) $T^2$ is Walsh-flat (Sec.~7), an event the permutation makes likely once $\min(\varepsilon^2 K_4,\varepsilon K_\infty)\ge C\log(K/\delta)$ (Fig.~6). Retain a positivity/offset margin, and choose the gain window $W$ from the bias--variance tradeoff of the proposed windowed estimator. Under those checks, the proposed acquisition rule buys statistical self-calibration without surrendering orthogonal conditioning.

\noindent\textbf{A phase diagram as a falsifiable design map.} The master finite-noise identity (Theorem~1) and the finite-$N$ flip boundary $\rho^*$ (Prop~3) render relMSE across DGI, SRHT, and pairwise-Hadamard as a chart over basis, drift, and noise (Fig.~5). It is worth noting that $\rho_{\mathrm{pair}}$ (adjacent-pair decorrelation, entering $\rho^*$) and $\rho_{\mathrm{bw}}$ (bandwidth, entering the tall-design law) are distinct axes and must be labeled as such before any overlay (Remark, Sec.~8). The heuristic $\rho^*\approx 2C_0(\mathrm{pipeline})/(NK_{\mathrm{eff}}s^2)$ appears as the leading-order limit of the full boundary --- a prediction the diagram exposes to falsification, not a fitted curve.

\noindent\textbf{A cross-domain principle.} The exportable statement is basis-agnostic: \emph{to self-calibrate a multiplicative nuisance, design the carrier so its distribution is stationary in the nuisance coordinate.} The following are offered as analogues and motivation, not as proven domains --- each would require its own operator analysis of the diagonal-row map \cite{LiLeeBresler1501.06120,KechKrahmer1603.07316}: blind gain and phase calibration, flat-field correction, coded-aperture and computational microscopy, and single-pixel spectroscopy all share the bilinear structure in which this reasoning applies.

\noindent\textbf{Scope and limits.} The claims are theory and simulation only, with no hardware validation. Uniqueness near $N=K+p$ is an identifiability statement, not a conditioning guarantee; stability there is a separate random-matrix question. Random square unconstrained designs are \emph{not} algebraically identifiable --- any invertible design absorbs nonconstant gain into a redefined object (Theorem~A). There is no universal DGI constant $C_0$ or Hadamard factor $D_H$; both must be measured per pipeline. The low-photon soft-log $\psi_\alpha$ avoids the log-zero singularity but cannot manufacture information --- zero photons carry zero Fisher information for log-intensity, an information limit, not an estimator defect.

\noindent\textbf{Experimental feasibility.} The theory issues sharp, falsifiable predictions for a bench with programmable drift: a drift-rate flip point $\rho^*$ at which randomized carriers overtake pairwise Hadamard, and an SRHT-versus-ordered-Hadamard separation in blind gain-estimation error under stirred-scattering gain (Fig.~3). Testing these against a physical single-pixel system --- with real read noise, Poisson statistics, and unmodeled gain jumps --- is left to future hardware work, likely in collaboration with an optical-imaging group.

In conclusion, we have established an identifiability theory of blind gain--object separation for single-pixel acquisition: the square-design obstruction and the tall-design thresholds (Theorems A and A$'$), the stationarity anchor with its minimax-rate-optimal windowed estimator (Theorems B--D), the SRHT synthesis (Sec.~7), and the finite-noise reconstruction bridge with its flip-boundary phase diagram (Theorem~1, Prop~3). Taken together, these tools make gain observable not by measuring it directly, but by making the object stop pretending to be time. The proposed framework opens an avenue for self-calibrating acquisition design in real-world scenarios, e.g., single-pixel imaging through dynamic and complex scattering media.
