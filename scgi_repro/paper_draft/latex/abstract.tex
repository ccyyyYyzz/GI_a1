\begin{abstract}
For a fixed square linear inverse with known gain and matched acquisition budget, an ordered orthogonal scan minimizes conditioning-driven amplification of additive noise; with unknown slow multiplicative gain and unconstrained objects, the same deterministic chronology can create an exact gain--object ambiguity --- the ordering hands the object a temporal signature that a slow gain can imitate exactly. We resolve this paradox with an identifiability theory of blind gain--object separation, distinguishing algebraic identifiability, statistical identifiability, and estimation conditioning. Every square invertible design fails algebraically --- for unconstrained objects, nonzero carrier entries, and admissible nonconstant log-gain perturbations, modulo the global scale; tall designs restore uniqueness generically at $N \ge K+p$ frames for a $p$-dimensional drift class. Independently, a stationarity anchor (Theorem~B) pins the relative gain statistically: once the object carrier is stationary in time, slow modulation of the record can only be gain. Randomized Hadamard/SRHT acquisition preserves orthogonal conditioning, and under Walsh-flatness or a successful random permutation of a sufficiently spread object it supplies the stationary or exchangeable carrier needed by the windowed gain estimator; an exact finite-noise relative-MSE identity then yields a phase diagram with a computable flip boundary between ordered and randomized acquisition. In matched-budget simulations, raw ordered Hadamard leaves a non-decaying blind gain error near 0.96 where randomized arms reach 0.02, and SRHT recovers $+5.45$ dB under slow drift (descriptive values on the simulation grid). The design rule: randomize the carrier's chronology --- make the object stop pretending to be time.
\end{abstract}
